A **roof** (pl.: **roofs** or **rooves**) is the top covering of a [building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building "Building"), including all materials and constructions necessary to support it on the walls of the building or on uprights, providing protection against [rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain "Rain"), [snow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snow "Snow"), [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight"), extremes of [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), and [wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind "Wind").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-1) A roof is part of the [building envelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_envelope "Building envelope").
The characteristics of a roof are dependent upon the purpose of the building that it covers, the available roofing materials and the local traditions of [construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction "Construction") and wider concepts of [architectural design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_design "Architectural design") and practice, and may also be governed by local or national [legislation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislation "Legislation"). In most countries, a roof protects primarily against rain. A [verandah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verandah "Verandah") may be roofed with material that protects against sunlight but admits the other elements. The roof of a [garden conservatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatory_\(greenhouse\) "Conservatory \(greenhouse\)") protects plants from cold, wind, and rain, but admits light.
A roof may also provide additional living space, for example, a [roof garden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_garden "Roof garden").
Old English _hrof_[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-2) 'roof, [ceiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceiling "Ceiling"), top, summit; heaven, sky', also figuratively, 'highest point of something', from [Proto-Germanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Germanic "Proto-Germanic") *_khrofam_ ([cf.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cf. "Cf.") [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_language "Dutch language") _roef_ 'deckhouse, cabin, coffin-lid', [Middle High German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_High_German "Middle High German") _rof_ 'penthouse', Old Norse _hrof_ 'boat shed'). There are no apparent connections outside the Germanic family. "English alone has retained the word in a general sense, for which the other languages use forms corresponding to OE. _þæc_ thatch".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-3)
* the [construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_roof_construction "Domestic roof construction")
The **material** of a roof may range from [banana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banana "Banana") leaves, wheaten [straw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straw "Straw") or [seagrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seagrass "Seagrass") to [laminated glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_glass "Architectural glass"), [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") _(see:[copper roofing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper_in_architecture#Roofing "Copper in architecture"))_, [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") sheeting and [pre-cast concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-cast_concrete "Pre-cast concrete"). In many parts of the world [ceramic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic "Ceramic") [roof tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_tiles "Roof tiles") have been the predominant roofing material for centuries, if not millennia. Other roofing materials include [asphalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitumen "Bitumen"), [coal tar pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_tar "Coal tar"), [EPDM rubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EPDM_rubber "EPDM rubber"), [Hypalon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypalon "Hypalon"), [polyurethane foam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_polyurethane_applications "List of polyurethane applications"), [PVC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride"), [slate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slate "Slate"), [Teflon fabric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene "Polytetrafluoroethylene"), [TPO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoplastic_olefin "Thermoplastic olefin"), and [wood shakes and shingles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood_shingle "Wood shingle").
The **construction** of a roof is determined by its method of support and how the underneath space is bridged and whether or not the roof is _pitched_. The _[pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_pitch "Roof pitch")_ is the angle at which the roof rises from its lowest to its highest point. Most US [domestic architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House"), except in very dry regions, has roofs that are sloped, or _pitched_. Although modern construction elements such as drainpipes may remove the need for pitch, roofs are pitched for reasons of tradition and aesthetics. So the pitch is partly dependent upon stylistic factors, and partially to do with practicalities.
Some types of roofing, for example [thatch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thatch "Thatch"), require a steep pitch in order to be waterproof and durable. Other types of roofing, for example [pantiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantile_\(roof_material\) "Pantile \(roof material\)"), are unstable on a steeply pitched roof but provide excellent weather protection at a relatively low angle. In regions where there is little rain, an almost flat roof with a slight run-off provides adequate protection against an occasional downpour. Drainpipes also remove the need for a sloping roof.
A person that specializes in roof construction is called a [roofer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roofer "Roofer").

The **shape of roofs** differs greatly from region to region. The main factors which influence the shape of roofs are the climate and the materials available for roof structure and the outer covering.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-4)
The basic shapes of roofs are [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_roof "Flat roof"), [mono-pitched](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mono-pitched_roof "Mono-pitched roof"), [gabled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gable "Gable"), [mansard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansard_roof "Mansard roof"), [hipped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hip_roof "Hip roof"), [butterfly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly_roof "Butterfly roof"), [arched](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch "Arch") and [domed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dome "Dome"). There are many variations on these types. Roofs constructed of flat sections that are sloped are referred to as [pitched roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitched_roof "Pitched roof") (generally if the angle exceeds 10 degrees).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-5) Pitched roofs, including gabled, hipped and skillion roofs, make up the greatest number of domestic roofs. Some roofs follow organic shapes, either by architectural design or because a flexible material such as thatch has been used in the construction.
The roof structure is generally supported upon walls, although some building styles, for example, [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_dome "Geodesic dome") and [A-frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A-frame_house "A-frame house"), blur the distinction between wall and roof.
Main article: [Domestic roof construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_roof_construction "Domestic roof construction")
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Linkopings_stadsbibliotek_roof2.jpg/250px-Linkopings_stadsbibliotek_roof2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Linkopings_stadsbibliotek_roof2.jpg)The roof of a library in [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden")
The supporting structure of a roof usually comprises beams that are long and of strong, fairly rigid material such as [timber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timber "Timber"), and since the mid-19th century, [cast iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cast_iron "Cast iron") or [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel"). In countries that use [bamboo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo "Bamboo") extensively, the flexibility of the material causes a distinctive curving line to the roof, characteristic of [Oriental architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_architecture "Japanese architecture").
Stone [lintels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_and_lintel "Post and lintel") have been used to support roofs since [prehistoric times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory "Prehistory"), but cannot bridge large distances. The stone arch came into extensive use in the [ancient Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") period and in variant forms could be used to span spaces up to 45 m (140 ft) across. The stone arch or [vault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vault_\(architecture\) "Vault \(architecture\)"), with or without ribs, dominated the roof structures of major architectural works for about 2,000 years, only giving way to iron beams with the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution") and the designing of such buildings as [Paxton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Paxton "Joseph Paxton")'s [Crystal Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace "The Crystal Palace"), completed 1851.
With continual improvements in [steel girders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel_girder "Steel girder"), these became the major structural support for large roofs, and eventually for ordinary houses as well. Another form of [girder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girder "Girder") is the [reinforced concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinforced_concrete "Reinforced concrete") beam, in which metal rods are encased in concrete, giving it greater strength under [tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tension_\(physics\) "Tension \(physics\)").
Main article: [List of commercially available roofing material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_commercially_available_roofing_material "List of commercially available roofing material")
This part of the roof shows great variation dependent upon availability of material. In [vernacular architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernacular_architecture "Vernacular architecture"), roofing material is often vegetation, such as [thatches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thatching "Thatching"), the most durable being [sea grass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_grass "Sea grass") with a life of perhaps 40 years. In many Asian countries bamboo is used both for the supporting structure and the outer layer where split bamboo stems are laid turned alternately and overlapped. In areas with an abundance of timber, wooden [shingles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_shingle "Roof shingle"), shakes and [boards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_roof "Board roof") are used, while in some countries the [bark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch-bark_roof "Birch-bark roof") of certain trees can be peeled off in thick, heavy sheets and used for roofing.
The 20th century saw the manufacture of composition [asphalt shingles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphalt_shingle "Asphalt shingle") which can last from a thin 20-year shingle to the thickest which are limited lifetime shingles, the cost depending on the thickness and durability of the shingle. When a layer of shingles wears out, they are usually stripped, along with the underlay and roofing nails, allowing a new layer to be installed. An alternative method is to install another layer directly over the worn layer. While this method is faster, it does not allow the roof sheathing to be inspected and water damage, often associated with worn shingles, to be repaired. Having multiple layers of old shingles under a new layer causes roofing nails to be located further from the sheathing, weakening their hold. The greatest concern with this method is that the weight of the extra material could exceed the dead load capacity of the roof structure and cause collapse. Because of this, jurisdictions which use the [International Building Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Building_Code "International Building Code") prohibit the installation of new roofing on top of an existing roof that has two or more applications of any type of roof covering; the existing roofing material must be removed before installing a new roof.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-6)
[Slate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slate "Slate") is an ideal, and durable material, while in the [Swiss Alps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Alps "Swiss Alps") roofs are made from huge slabs of stone, several inches thick. The slate roof is often considered the best type of roofing. A slate roof may last 75 to 150 years, and even longer. However, slate roofs are often expensive to install – in the US, for example, a slate roof may have the same cost as the rest of the house. Often, the first part of a slate roof to fail is the fixing nails; they corrode, allowing the slates to slip. In the UK, this condition is known as "nail sickness". Because of this problem, fixing nails made of [stainless steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stainless_steel "Stainless steel") or [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") are recommended, and even these must be protected from the weather.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-7)
[Asbestos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asbestos "Asbestos"), usually in bonded corrugated panels, has been used widely in the 20th century as an inexpensive, non-flammable roofing material with excellent insulating properties. [Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asbestosis "Asbestosis") and [legal issues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asbestos_and_the_law "Asbestos and the law") involved in the mining and handling of asbestos products means that it is no longer used as a new roofing material. However, many asbestos roofs continue to exist, particularly in South America and Asia.
Roofs made of cut turf (modern ones known as [green roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_roof "Green roof"), traditional ones as [sod roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sod_roof "Sod roof")) have good insulating properties and are increasingly encouraged as a way of "greening" the Earth. The soil and vegetation function as living insulation, moderating building temperatures.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-8) Adobe roofs are roofs of clay, mixed with binding material such as straw or animal hair, and plastered on lathes to form a flat or gently sloped roof, usually in areas of low rainfall.
In areas where clay is plentiful, roofs of baked [tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_tiles "Roof tiles") have been the major form of roofing. The casting and firing of roof tiles is an industry that is often associated with [brickworks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brickworks "Brickworks"). While the shape and colour of tiles was once regionally distinctive, now tiles of many shapes and colours are produced commercially, to suit the taste and pocketbook of the purchaser. Concrete roof tiles are also a common choice, being available in many different styles and shapes.
[Sheet metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural_metals "Architectural metals") in the form of [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") and [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead") has also been used for many hundreds of years. Both are expensive but durable, the vast copper roof of [Chartres Cathedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral "Chartres Cathedral"), oxidised to a pale green colour, having been in place for hundreds of years. Lead, which is sometimes used for church roofs, was most commonly used as [flashing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashing_\(weatherproofing\) "Flashing \(weatherproofing\)") in valleys and around chimneys on domestic roofs, particularly those of slate. Copper was used for the same purpose.
In the 19th century, iron, electroplated with [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc") to improve its resistance to rust, became a light-weight, easily transported, waterproofing material. Its low cost and easy application made it the most accessible commercial roofing, worldwide. Since then, many types of [metal roofing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_roof "Metal roof") have been developed. Steel shingle or standing-seam roofs last about 50 years or more depending on both the method of installation and the moisture barrier (underlayment) used and are between the cost of shingle roofs and slate roofs. In the 20th century, a large number of roofing materials were developed, including roofs based on bitumen (already used in previous centuries), on rubber and on a range of synthetics such as [thermoplastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoplastic "Thermoplastic") and on [fibreglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibreglass "Fibreglass").

Some roofing materials, particularly those of natural fibrous material, such as thatch, have excellent insulating properties. For those that do not, extra [insulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_insulation "Building insulation") is often installed under the outer layer. In developed countries, the majority of dwellings have a [ceiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceiling "Ceiling") installed under the structural members of the roof. The purpose of a ceiling is to insulate against heat and cold, noise, dirt and often from the droppings and lice of birds who frequently choose roofs as nesting places.
Forms of insulation are felt or plastic sheeting, sometimes with a reflective surface, installed directly below the tiles or other material; synthetic foam batting laid above the ceiling and recycled paper products and other such materials that can be inserted or sprayed into roof cavities. [Cool roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cool_roof "Cool roof") are becoming increasingly popular,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-10.3390/su13094665-9) and in some cases are mandated by local codes. Cool roofs are defined as roofs with both high [reflectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectivity "Reflectivity") and high [thermal emittance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_emittance "Thermal emittance").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-10.3390/su13094665-9)
Poorly insulated and ventilated roofing can suffer from problems such as the formation of [ice dams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_dam_\(roof\) "Ice dam \(roof\)") around the overhanging eaves in cold weather, causing water from melted snow on upper parts of the roof to penetrate the roofing material. Ice dams occur when heat escapes through the uppermost part of the roof, and the snow at those points melts, refreezing as it drips along the shingles, and collecting in the form of ice at the lower points. This can result in structural damage from stress, including the destruction of gutter and drainage systems.
While flat roofs are more prone to drainage issues, poorly designed or textured sloping roofs can face similar problems.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-10) Standing water on a roof can lead to [mold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mold "Mold") growth, which is highly damaging to both the building’s structure and the health of its occupants. Repairing drainage issues is significantly less costly than fixing the damage caused by [mold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mold "Mold").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof#cite_note-11)
In general, the pitch of the roof is proportional to the amount of precipitation. Houses in areas of low rainfall frequently have roofs of low pitch while those in areas of high rainfall and snow, have steep roofs. The [longhouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longhouse "Longhouse") of [Papua New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea "Papua New Guinea"), for example, being roof-dominated architecture, the high roofs sweeping almost to the ground. The high steeply pitched roofs of Germany and Holland are typical in regions of snowfall. In parts of North America such as [Buffalo, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York "Buffalo, New York"), United States, or [Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal "Montreal"), Quebec, Canada, there is a required minimum slope of 6 in 12 (1:2, a pitch of 30°).
The water repelled by the roof during a rainstorm is potentially damaging to the building that the roof protects. If it runs down the walls, it may seep into the mortar or through panels. If it lies around the foundations it may cause seepage to the interior, [rising damp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rising_damp_\(structural\) "Rising damp \(structural\)") or [dry rot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_rot "Dry rot"). For this reason most buildings have a system in place to protect the walls of a building from most of the roof water. Overhanging [eaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eaves "Eaves") are commonly employed for this purpose. Most modern roofs and many old ones have systems of valleys, gutters, waterspouts, waterheads and [drainpipes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drain_\(plumbing\) "Drain \(plumbing\)") to remove the water from the vicinity of the building. In many parts of the world, roofwater is collected and stored for domestic use.

Newer systems include [solar shingles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_shingle "Solar shingle") which generate [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity") as well as cover the roof. There are also solar systems available that generate hot water or hot air and which can also act as a roof covering. More complex systems may carry out all of these functions: generate electricity, recover thermal energy, and also act as a roof covering.
Solar systems can be integrated with roofs by:      integration in the covering of pitched roofs, e.g. solar shingles,     mounting on an existing roof, e.g. [solar panel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photovoltaic_module "Photovoltaic module") on a [tile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tile "Tile") roof,     integration in a flat roof membrane using heat welding (e.g. PVC) or     mounting on a flat roof with a construction and additional weight to prevent uplift from wind.
