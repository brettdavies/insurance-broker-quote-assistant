Why Uninsured Motorist Coverage is Non-Negotiable in California

We recommend a minimum of $100,000 in Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) coverage, but carrying $250,000 is a safer bet if you can afford it.

Hereâs why: California has one of the highest costs of living in the United States, yet our required auto insurance liability limits are among the lowest. The state only requires drivers to carry a mere $15,000 for the injury or death of one person.

What does $15,000 actually cover after a moderate to serious accident? Not much. Consider these average costs our clients face after a crash in California:

If the at-fault driver only has the minimum coverage, their policy will be exhausted quickly, leaving you to pay the rest out-of-pocket.

The High Cost of Inadequate Coverage

Choosing to save a few dollars by skimping on UM/UIM coverage is one of the biggest financial risks a driver can take. Without it, you could face:

  • Mounting Medical Debt: Your health insurance may cover some initial costs, but you will likely be responsible for deductibles, co-pays, and services that arenât covered.
  • Reimbursing Your Health Insurer: If you receive a settlement from the at-fault driver, your health insurance company has a right to be reimbursed. After paying them back from a meager $15,000 settlement, you could be left with nothing for your pain and suffering.
  • No Compensation for Pain and Suffering: A small liability policy leaves no room to compensate you for the physical pain and emotional distress caused by the accident.
  • Inability to Recover Lost Wages: If you canât work due to your injuries, you could lose your income with no way to recover it from the underinsured driver.

Donât Forget Property Damage (UMPD)

While your medical bills are the top priority, what about your car? If an uninsured driver hits you, you could be left without a vehicle and no way to pay for repairs. This is where Uninsured Motorist Property Damage (UMPD) coverage comes in.

UMPD is a separate coverage that pays for repairs to your vehicle if itâs damaged by a driver with no insurance. In California, UMPD coverage has a limit of $3,500.

Key things to know about UMPD:

  • Itâs for the uninsured, not underinsured: UMPD typically only applies when the at-fault driver is completely uninsured.
  • The deductible is lower: For many, the main benefit is the lower deductible. UMPD deductibles are often much smaller than standard collision deductibles.

If you donât have collision coverage, adding UMPD is an absolute must. If you do have collision, weigh the cost against the benefit of a lower deductible in an uninsured driver scenario.

UM/UIM vs. Liability vs. Collision Coverage

Insurance policies can be confusing. Many drivers mistakenly believe âfull coverageâ automatically protects them from everything. Hereâs a clear breakdown of who and what is covered by these key policy types.

Coverage Type What It Covers Who It Pays For When Itâs Used
Bodily Injury Liability Medical bills, lost wages, and pain & suffering for others. Other people you injure in an accident. When you are at fault for the accident.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Weâve compiled answers to the most common questions about uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage in California.

1. Do I need uninsured motorist coverage if I have full coverage?
Yes. âFull coverageâ typically refers to having liability, collision, and comprehensive insurance. It does not automatically include Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM) coverage. You must specifically add it to your policy.
2. Is uninsured motorist coverage required in California?
No, California does not legally require drivers to carry UM/UIM coverage, which is why itâs so important to proactively add it to your policy to protect yourself.
3. What does uninsured motorist bodily injury (UMBI) cover?
Uninsured Motorist Bodily Injury (UMBI) covers your medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering if you are injured in an accident caused by a driver who has no insurance. Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage applies when the at-fault driver has insurance, but their policy limits arenât high enough to cover all your damages.
4. Will making an uninsured motorist claim raise my insurance rates?
In California, thanks to Proposition 103, your insurance company is legally forbidden from raising your rates for making a UM/UIM claim after an accident that was not your fault.
5. What should my uninsured motorist coverage limits be?
We recommend at least $100,000/$300,000 (the first number is the per-person limit, the second is the per-accident limit), but $250,000/$500,000 is highly recommended if you can afford it. Note that most umbrella insurance policies require you to have $250,000 in underlying UM/UIM coverage.
