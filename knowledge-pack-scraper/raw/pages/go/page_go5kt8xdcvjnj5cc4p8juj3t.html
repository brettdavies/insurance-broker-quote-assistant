Florida’s 15-Year Roof Rule Explained

Florida’s 15-Year Roof Rule is an insurance regulation designed to protect homeowners from being unfairly denied coverage due to roof age alone. The rule, part of Florida Statute 627.7011, prevents insurers from refusing to issue or renew a homeowner’s policy simply because a roof is under 15 years old. If your roof is 15 years old or older, your insurance company may require a roof inspection before renewal or issuing a new policy. If the inspection certifies that your roof still has at least five years of useful life, the insurer cannot deny coverage based solely on its age.

This rule helps balance homeowner protection with safety standards—ensuring older but well-maintained roofs remain insurable while limiting risk for insurers.

What the 15-Year Roof Rule Means for Florida Homeowners

  • Roofs under 15 years old: Insurance companies cannot deny coverage or non-renew policies due to age alone.

  • Roofs 15 years or older: Homeowners must provide proof—typically via an inspection report—that the roof has at least five more years of life remaining.

  • Replacement not required: The law does not force homeowners to replace a roof simply because it’s over 15 years old.

  • Insurance pricing impact: Some insurers may still increase premiums or limit coverage based on roof material or inspection results.

Key Takeaways from Florida Statute 627.7011

  • Insurers may not cancel or refuse renewal if your roof is less than 15 years old.

  • For roofs over 15 years, insurers must allow inspection and certification before non-renewal.

  • Five years of certified life is the threshold for continued insurability.

  • The rule aims to reduce unnecessary roof replacements and stabilize insurance markets after years of rising costs and lawsuits.

Tips for Florida First Time Homeowners

  1. Get a roof inspection every few years after the 10-year mark.

  2. Keep repair records—they help prove your roof’s remaining lifespan.

  3. Work with licensed inspectors familiar with Florida’s roof certification forms.

  4. Shop around—not all insurers treat older roofs equally.
