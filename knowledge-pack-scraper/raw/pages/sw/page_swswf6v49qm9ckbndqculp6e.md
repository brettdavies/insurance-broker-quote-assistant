# Hail Mary: Preparing for Texas’s Fiercest Storms
  * [ April 17, 2025 ](https://insuranceclaimrecoverysupport.com/2025/04/17/)
  * 9:05 am
  * [ Scott Friedson  ](https://insuranceclaimrecoverysupport.com/author/scott-friedson/)


## The Rising Threat of Hail Storms in Texas
**Hail storm Texas** events have become increasingly severe and frequent in recent years, causing billions of dollars in property damage across the state. If you’re concerned about hail storms in Texas, here’s what you need to know:
  * **Peak Season** : April through June, with March and July also seeing significant activity
  * **Most Affected Areas** : Dallas-Fort Worth, San Antonio, Austin, and the “Hail Alley” region
  * **Recent Impacts** : The March 2025 storm in Kerrville produced baseball-sized hail that lasted up to 25 minutes
  * **Average Damage** : Texas ranks 1st nationally with annual property damage of $2,207.73 per 100 residents


Texas experienced a staggering 1,243 hailstorms from 2012 to 2021, with some recent events causing up to $2 billion in damages in a single occurrence. These powerful storms can produce hail ranging from marble-sized to softball-sized, capable of shattering windshields, puncturing roofs, and causing extensive structural damage to buildings.
What makes Texas hail storms particularly challenging is that damage isn’t always immediately apparent. Many commercial and multifamily property owners find leaks and structural problems months or even years after a hail event, when insurance claims become more difficult to process.
I’m Scott Friedson, a multi-state licensed public adjuster who has personally handled hundreds of **hail storm Texas** insurance claims, helping property owners recover over $250 million in settlements following severe weather events. Having witnessed how destructive these storms can be, I’ll guide you through practical steps to prepare for and respond to Texas’s fiercest hail events.
## Understanding Hail Storms in Texas
When it comes to severe weather, Texas holds a special place in the meteorological record books. The Lone Star State sits at a unique crossroads where cold, dry air from the Rocky Mountains collides with warm, moist air from the Gulf of Mexico. This atmospheric dance creates perfect conditions for powerful thunderstorms that often pack a destructive punch in the form of hail.
“Texas had recorded a total of 1,243 hailstorms from 2012 to 2021,” according to comprehensive weather data analysis. This isn’t just an impressive statistic—it represents billions of dollars in property damage and countless disrupted lives across the state.
Much of Texas falls within what weather experts call **“Hail Alley”** – a region stretching from Texas through Oklahoma, Colorado, and Wyoming that experiences North America’s most frequent and severe hail events. This geographical reality has made **hail storm Texas** events a fact of life for residents.
The financial impact tells a sobering story. In 2017 alone, auto insurers paid over $3 billion in hail damage claims across Texas. More recently, Dallas-Fort Worth endured hail events in June 2023 that caused an estimated $7 to $8 billion in damage. San Antonio residents still remember the April 2016 hailstorm that resulted in $1.4 billion in damages in a single afternoon.
### Regional Impact Across Texas
Not all parts of Texas face equal risk when it comes to **hail storm Texas** events. The Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex has historically borne the brunt of some of the state’s most damaging hail. In 2012, Dallas County residents watched in horror as hailstones up to 3 inches in diameter—larger than baseballs—pummeled the area for approximately 30 minutes. Lakewood neighborhood suffered particularly severe damage during this relentless barrage.
Central Texas, including Austin, San Antonio, and the picturesque Hill Country, also experiences significant hail activity. The March 2025 hailstorm in Kerrville produced baseball-sized hail that repeatedly struck vehicles and homes. Local resident Scott Christensen summed it up simply: “I don’t think we’ve ever had anything quite like this.”
East Texas and the Panhandle region face their own hail challenges, though their seasonal patterns differ somewhat from Central and North Texas.
Region | Average Hail Size | Annual Frequency | Peak Season  
---|---|---|---  
North Texas (DFW) | 1-3 inches | 30-40 events | April-June  
Central Texas (Austin/San Antonio) | 1-2.5 inches | 25-35 events | March-June  
East Texas | 0.75-1.5 inches | 15-25 events | April-May  
West Texas/Panhandle | 1-2 inches | 20-30 events | May-July  
Gulf Coast | 0.5-1 inch | 10-15 events | March-April  
### Seasonal Patterns
While **hail storm Texas** events can technically occur any month of the year, they follow distinct seasonal patterns that property owners should understand. The peak hail season typically runs from **April through June** , with March and July also showing significant activity. During these months, atmospheric conditions create the perfect recipe for severe thunderstorms capable of producing substantial hail.
Andrew Brown, who lived through the March 2025 Kerrville hailstorm, described the terrifying experience: “It was incredibly loud. The video really doesn’t do it justice. It was really hard to hear anybody talk, just breaking through the skylights causing extensive damage.”
What makes Texas hail particularly concerning isn’t just its frequency but its unpredictability. Storms can develop with alarming speed, sometimes with limited warning time. This unpredictability increases when weather balloon launches—which provide critical data for accurate forecasting—are reduced due to budget constraints or other factors.
For Texas property owners, this combination of frequency, severity, and unpredictability makes preparation not just advisable but essential. Understanding your specific region’s risk factors and seasonal patterns is the first step toward protecting your property from these powerful storms that have become an unwelcome part of Texas life.
## Preparing Your Property for a Hail Storm
Let’s face it – when those dark clouds roll in over Texas, your home needs to be ready. After seeing hundreds of properties damaged by hail, I can tell you that a little preparation goes a long way. The good news? You don’t need to be a home improvement expert to protect your property from a **hail storm Texas** event.
### Roof Inspection and Maintenance
Your roof is your first defender against those ice pellets from the sky, and its condition can make or break your hail experience.
Think of your roof like you would your car – it needs regular check-ups. Schedule a professional inspection at least once yearly, ideally before March when our hail season kicks into gear. A qualified roofer can spot those trouble areas you might miss – loose shingles, weak spots, or aging materials that could fail when golf ball-sized hail comes calling.
Don’t put off replacing damaged materials. That small issue you’re ignoring could turn into a ceiling-sized problem during the next storm. And if you’re due for a full roof replacement, consider investing in impact-resistant materials. Class 4 impact-resistant shingles can stand up to 2-inch hailstones and might even lower your insurance premiums – a win-win!
Remember those gutters too. Clear drainage systems prevent water backup that can compound hail damage with water issues. A clogged gutter during a **hail storm Texas** event is like trying to bail water with a strainer – not effective!

### Securing Outdoor Items
When hail hits, ordinary objects become projectiles. That forgotten lawn chair? It could end up causing more damage than the hail itself.
Before storm season, develop a habit of securing outdoor items. Move patio furniture, grills, and planters to covered areas when storms threaten. For larger items that can’t be relocated, invest in quality weather-resistant covers or straps designed for Texas-sized winds.
**Regular tree maintenance** is another often-overlooked preparation step. Those beautiful oak branches hanging over your roof might look picturesque, but during a **hail storm Texas** event, they can become battering rams. Trim them back before they have a chance to cause damage.
Don’t forget about protecting fixed outdoor systems like air conditioning units and pool equipment. Protective covers are relatively inexpensive compared to replacing these systems after hail damage.
### Upgrading to Hail-Resistant Materials
If you’re building or renovating, think of it as an opportunity to hail-proof your property.
**Impact-resistant roofing** materials might cost more upfront, but they pay dividends during severe weather. Class 4 materials offer the highest protection level, potentially saving you thousands in future repairs.
**Metal roofing** is another excellent option. While it may show cosmetic denting after a severe storm, it typically won’t puncture or fail structurally like traditional materials might.
For your home’s exterior walls, **fiber cement siding** stands up to hail better than vinyl or wood alternatives. It’s more durable and requires less maintenance over time.
Windows are often overlooked in hail preparation, but **impact-resistant glass** or storm shutters can prevent dangerous and costly breakage during severe storms.
And let’s not forget about protecting vehicles. If you’re building or renovating, consider adding **covered parking structures**. A simple carport can save you from a dented vehicle and the headache of dealing with auto insurance after a storm.

Being prepared for a **hail storm Texas** event means thinking through every vulnerable part of your property. Here’s what needs to be on your radar:

## Safety Measures During a Hail Storm
When dark clouds gather and the forecast calls for a **hail storm Texas** event, your focus needs to shift quickly from protecting property to ensuring personal safety. Those innocent-looking ice pellets can become dangerous projectiles in seconds, sometimes reaching the size of baseballs in severe Texas storms.
### Indoor Safety Protocols
The safest place during a hailstorm is inside a sturdy building, away from anything that might shatter. During the fierce March 2025 Kerrville hailstorm, Andrew Brown and his family made a smart decision to take shelter in a Home Depot when the sky darkened. He later shared, “It could have been way worse had we been on the road,” a sobering reminder of the protection solid structures provide.
When you’re indoors during a **hail storm Texas** event, head to interior rooms without exterior walls or windows. Bathrooms, hallways, or closets near the center of your home offer the best protection. Stay well away from windows, as hailstones can shatter glass and send dangerous shards flying through the air. Drawing blinds or curtains provides an extra layer of protection by helping contain broken glass.
For extra peace of mind during particularly severe storms, keep mattresses or heavy blankets handy. These can provide additional protection if you’re concerned about roof damage or flying debris. And don’t forget about your little ones – make sure children and pets understand the importance of staying away from windows when those ice chunks start falling.

### Monitoring Weather Alerts
In Texas, weather conditions can change dramatically in minutes. Staying informed about approaching storms gives you precious time to prepare and take shelter:
The National Weather Service recommends having at least two ways to receive severe weather warnings – perhaps a combination of weather apps, NOAA weather radio, local TV and radio stations, or outdoor warning sirens. Understanding the difference between a watch (conditions are favorable for severe storms) and a warning (severe weather has been spotted) can make all the difference in your response time.
When meteorologists mention hail sizes, they’re giving you valuable information about the storm’s severity. Pea-sized hail might sting a bit, but baseball-sized hail can be life-threatening. Following trusted local meteorologists on social media can provide you with the most timely and location-specific information when storms approach.
### Emergency Kit Essentials
Every Texas home should have a well-stocked emergency kit ready for severe weather events. Your kit doesn’t need to be elaborate, but should include essentials like flashlights with extra batteries, first aid supplies, and bottled water (one gallon per person per day is the standard recommendation).
Don’t forget to pack non-perishable food, a battery-powered radio, cell phone chargers with backup batteries, and any important medications your family needs. Including emergency contact information, waterproof copies of important documents, and some cash in small denominations can be invaluable if you need to evacuate.
For **hail storm Texas** events specifically, consider adding tools for emergency repairs like tarps, duct tape, and plastic sheeting. These can help you quickly cover broken windows or roof damage until professional help arrives.
### Staying Safe During a Hail Storm Texas
If you’re caught outdoors when hail begins to fall, don’t panic – but do act quickly. Seek substantial shelter immediately, looking for the nearest sturdy building. Shopping centers, schools, or public buildings make excellent temporary shelters if you’re away from home.
If shelter isn’t immediately available, use your hands and arms to protect your head and face. Even a backpack or jacket held over your head can provide some protection from smaller hailstones. Never try to outrun a storm – this is a dangerous mistake many people make. Instead, if you’re driving, pull over safely, stay in your vehicle, and lie down if possible, protecting your face from potential broken glass.
Hailstorms often come in waves, so wait until you’re certain the storm has completely passed before venturing outside again. That brief pause between waves of hail has fooled many Texans into leaving shelter too early.
### Protecting Your Vehicle During a Hail Storm Texas
Vehicles are particularly vulnerable to hail damage, with repair costs often running into thousands of dollars. During a severe **hail storm Texas** event in 2023, a meteorologist offered this simple but valuable advice: “If you can, park your car under a covering.” This straightforward recommendation can save you significant headaches and expense.
Whenever possible, park under cover – garages, carports, or parking structures provide the best protection, especially during hail season. If you live in a hail-prone area of Texas, specialized car covers designed for hail protection can be a worthwhile investment.
In an emergency situation without proper cover, improvise with what you have. Thick blankets or even car floor mats placed over the windshield and hood can provide some protection. If you’re driving when a severe storm approaches, look for covered parking or a parking garage where you can wait it out safely.
If no cover is available and you’re stuck, try positioning your car so the windshield faces the direction of the hail, as windshields are designed to be more impact-resistant than side windows. Though – your safety matters more than your vehicle. Never risk personal injury trying to protect your car during an active hailstorm.
For the latest research on hail safety and preparation guidelines, the [National Weather Service](https://www.weather.gov/safety/thunderstorm-prepare) provides regularly updated resources that can help you stay safe during these unpredictable Texas weather events.
## What to Do After a Hail Storm
After a **hail storm Texas** event has passed, quick but careful action is essential to assess damage and start the recovery process. As public adjusters who’ve guided hundreds of property owners through post-storm challenges, I’ve seen how a systematic approach makes all the difference.
### Assessing and Documenting Damage
The moments after a hailstorm are critical for documentation—and proper evidence can make thousands of dollars of difference in your insurance settlement.
Safety must come first. Before you start inspecting your property, check for hazards like downed power lines, broken glass, or structural damage that could put you at risk. Once it’s safe, grab your phone or camera and start documenting everything.
Be methodical in your approach. Start with wide shots of entire areas—your roof, each side of your home, your vehicles—then move in for detailed close-ups of specific damage points. Don’t just focus on obvious damage; hail often causes subtle impacts that might not be immediately visible but can lead to serious problems later.
I remember working with a Dallas homeowner who almost missed significant damage to her north-facing roof because it wasn’t as obvious as the damage on the south side. Those hidden dents eventually led to leaks six months later, but because we’d documented everything thoroughly, her insurance claim covered the full repair.
Take detailed notes about the storm itself—when it happened, how long it lasted, and the approximate size of the hailstones. If possible, save a few hailstones in your freezer after photographing them with a ruler for scale. These details might seem trivial now, but they can become crucial evidence when working with your insurance company.
Create a thorough inventory of all damaged items, noting their age, condition, and estimated value. This documentation forms the foundation of your insurance claim, so be as comprehensive as possible.
### Emergency Temporary Repairs
Sometimes you can’t wait for the insurance process to play out before addressing immediate problems. Leaking roofs, broken windows, and water intrusion need prompt attention to prevent further damage.
For roof damage, secure tarps with boards (not just nails, which can cause more damage) to create a temporary waterproof barrier. Cover broken windows with plywood or heavy plastic sheeting. If water has already entered your home, remove it quickly to prevent mold growth.
Keep every receipt for these emergency repairs and supplies—these expenses are typically reimbursable under your policy. Just remember to focus only on temporary measures that prevent further damage. Hold off on permanent repairs until your insurance company has properly assessed the situation.
### Contacting Your Insurance Company
The insurance claim process after a **hail storm Texas** event can be complex, but approaching it systematically helps ensure fair treatment.
Contact your insurance company as soon as possible—most policies have time limits for reporting claims, sometimes as short as one year from the date of the storm. Request a complete copy of your policy if you don’t have one, and take time to understand your coverage limits, deductibles, and any exclusions.
Document every interaction with your insurance company. Note the date, time, who you spoke with, and what was discussed. This paper trail can be invaluable if disputes arise later.
When the insurance adjuster visits, be present to point out all damage. Consider having a contractor or public adjuster there as well to provide expert perspective. Insurance adjusters are human—they can miss things, especially subtle damage that might worsen over time.
Review any damage estimate or settlement offer carefully before accepting it. Once you sign off on a settlement, it can be difficult to reopen the claim if additional damage is finded later.
Be aware that insurance companies increasingly try to classify hail damage as “cosmetic” rather than “functional,” especially for metal roofs or siding. This distinction matters because many policies now include endorsements excluding coverage for cosmetic damage. A dented metal roof might still be waterproof today, but those dents can reduce its lifespan and should be addressed in your claim.

## Frequently Asked Questions about Hail Storms in Texas
### When Is Hail Season in Texas?
If you’re wondering when to be most vigilant about **hail storm Texas** events, mark your calendar from March through June. April and May typically see the most intense hail activity across the state, though Mother Nature doesn’t always follow our calendars – hail can surprise Texans during any month of the year.
The timing does shift a bit depending on where you call home in the Lone Star State. North Texas residents should be especially watchful in April and May, while Central Texans often see their worst hail slightly earlier, from March through May. If you’re up in the Panhandle or out in West Texas, your hail season peaks a bit later – typically May and June. Gulf Coast folks sometimes see early hail activity starting as soon as February.
Weather patterns are always in flux, and climate change appears to be reshaping traditional hail seasons. Some research suggests we may be trending toward less frequent but more intense hail events – meaning fewer storms, but potentially more damaging ones when they do occur. This makes staying weather-aware throughout spring and early summer all the more important.
### How Big Can Hail Get in Texas?
Texas doesn’t do anything small – including hail. While most hailstones are relatively modest pea-sized (¼ inch) to quarter-sized (1 inch) ice chunks, Texas regularly experiences the kind of hail that makes national news.
**Golf ball-sized hail** (1.75 inches) is unfortunately common during our severe spring storms. More concerning is that **baseball-sized hail** (2.75 inches) occurs multiple times annually somewhere in our state. The truly terrifying **softball-sized hail** (4+ inches) is rarer but has been well-documented across Texas.
The recent March 2025 Kerrville hailstorm that I mentioned earlier produced hailstones up to baseball size, battering homes and vehicles for an unusually long duration. The largest officially measured hailstone in Texas history measured nearly 6 inches in diameter – about the size of a DVD. There have been unofficial reports of even larger ice bombs during particularly violent storms.
What determines hail size is the strength of the thunderstorm’s updraft – essentially, how powerful the rising air current within the storm is. The stronger this updraft, the longer hailstones remain suspended in the freezing upper atmosphere, allowing them to grow larger before gravity finally wins the battle and sends them crashing down to earth.
### Does Home Insurance Cover Hail Damage?
The short answer is yes – most standard homeowners insurance policies in Texas do cover hail damage. But as with anything insurance-related, the details matter tremendously.
Your home’s structure (roof, siding, windows) is typically covered under the dwelling portion of your policy, while your personal belongings are protected under contents coverage. However, many Texas insurers have implemented special percentage-based deductibles specifically for hail and wind damage, usually ranging from 1% to 5% of your home’s insured value. 
What does this mean in practical terms? If you own a $300,000 home with a 2% wind/hail deductible, you’ll be responsible for the first $6,000 in damages before your insurance kicks in. That’s significantly higher than the fixed $500 or $1,000 deductibles many homeowners expect.
Another concerning trend is the addition of “cosmetic damage exclusions” in many policies. These clauses allow insurance companies to deny coverage for damage that affects only the appearance but not the function of your property. This distinction becomes particularly problematic with metal roofs, siding, and gutters – where dents may be classified as “merely cosmetic” even though they reduce your property’s value and potentially its lifespan.
Time limits present another challenge. Most policies require you to report damage within a specific timeframe, typically one year from the date of the storm. The problem? Hail damage to roofs can take months or even years to manifest as visible leaks, creating difficult situations for homeowners who find problems after the reporting window has closed.
Finally, be aware of whether your policy provides actual cash value coverage (which deducts for depreciation) versus replacement cost coverage, particularly for older roofs. This distinction can dramatically affect your settlement amount.
I always recommend reviewing your policy carefully before storm season hits. If you’re uncertain about your coverage, a consultation with a public adjuster can help you understand your policy’s fine print and identify potential gaps in protection.
### What Areas of Texas Get the Most Hail?
While **hail storm Texas** events can strike anywhere in our vast state, certain regions consistently find themselves in nature’s crosshairs:
The Dallas-Fort Worth Metroplex and North Texas region consistently rank among the most hail-prone areas not just in Texas, but in the entire United States. The unique combination of geographic location and atmospheric conditions creates the perfect storm, quite literally, for frequent and large hail.
Parts of the Texas Panhandle and West Texas fall within what meteorologists have dubbed “Hail Alley” – a corridor extending from Texas through Oklahoma, Colorado, and Wyoming that experiences North America’s most frequent hail events.
The Austin-San Antonio corridor and surrounding Hill Country experience significant hail activity, particularly during spring months, as evidenced by the devastating Kerrville storm earlier this year.
The Midland-Odessa region in West Texas sees frequent hail events, often associated with powerful supercell thunderstorms that develop over the plains.
East Texas, while experiencing somewhat fewer hailstorms than North and Central Texas, faces additional hazards when hail does strike due to the region’s dense tree cover, which creates falling branch hazards.
Insurance industry data confirms what Texans already know from experience – our state leads the nation in hail damage claims, with over 637,977 claims filed between just 2017 and 2019. Cities including Dallas, Fort Worth, San Antonio, Austin, and Amarillo consistently rank among the top U.S. cities for hail claims.
### How Long Do Hail Storms Typically Last?
The good news about **hail storm Texas** events is that they’re usually brief – typically lasting between 5 and 15 minutes in any specific location. The bad news? Even a few minutes of golf ball or baseball-sized hail can devastate your property.
The March 2025 Kerrville storm was unusually persistent, with reports indicating hail fell for 15 to 25 minutes – an exceptionally long duration that contributed to the widespread destruction across the community. This extended hailfall is one reason the damage was so severe.
The relatively brief nature of hailstorms is actually a protective factor – if hail were to fall for hours rather than minutes, the damage would be truly catastrophic. However, it’s important to note that while the actual hail precipitation at your specific location may be brief, the severe thunderstorm that produces it may affect your area for a longer period, bringing heavy rain, lightning, and high winds before and after the hail falls.
This is why weather awareness and having a safety plan are so important during Texas storm season. Those few minutes of hail can feel like an eternity when you’re listening to ice bombs pounding your roof, but knowing you’ve taken proper safety precautions can provide tremendous peace of mind.
