The last time liability limits in California increased was in 1967, even though it is by far the state with the most drivers and lowest minimum requirements on car insurance. Starting January 1, all car insurance policies will see a rate hike due to a new California liability limits increase to 30/60/15.

If you’re a California resident, you know that car insurance is already expensive, but the good news is that the new limits will hardly impact your wallet and you’ll be better protected if you cause an accident. Prior years’ liability limits were out of touch with how expensive car replacement, car parts and skilled labor have become ever since inflation hit a high. Medical costs are also at an all-time high and will not be decreasing. Here are the specifics in limit changes.

Key Takeaways

  • California’s new minimum liability limits will be 30/60/15 beginning January 2025.
  • Newer cars come with expensive sensors and driver assist technology that costs a bundle to fix and car replacement, repair and labor have increased exponentially.
  • Medical expenses are high and new bodily liability minimum requirements reflect current medical costs in California.
  • In 2035, the minimum liability limits for car insurance will be 50/100/25.

California Laws Pertaining to Liability Limits Increase in 2025

Bill 1107, also called the Protect California Drivers Act was signed by Governor Newsom in 2022. The bill allowed insurers to raise minimum auto insurance requirements and was set to begin January 1, 2025.

If you’ve seen an incremental increase in car insurance in January, now you know why. Formerly, liability limits were 15/3/5 and increased in 2025 to 30/60/15:

  • $30,000 bodily injury or death per person
  • $60,000 bodily injury or death per accident
  • $15,000 property damage per accident
  • Or, in lieu of coverage: $75,000 cash deposit with the DMV as proof of minimum financial responsibility (formerly $35,000).

If you thought that would be the end of it, 2035 will increase liability car insurance minimum requirements to 50/100/25.

What Is Liability Insurance?

Bodily Injury Liability Coverage for the At-fault Driver

In the event of an accident, if you cause injuries or death you’re covered up to $30,000 per person, $60,000 total per accident now for bodily injury. These figures are more in line with current medical costs.

Bodily injury liability insurance covers:

  • Hospital, doctor visits and rehabilitation costs related to physical injuries.
  • Pain and suffering.
  • Income loss due to injuries

Property Damage Liability Coverage for the At-fault Driver

In an accident, if you cause physical damage to the other car or cars, you’re covered up to $15,000 per accident. This figure includes

  • Cost to repair the other vehicle(s)
  • Cost to repair other damaged property, like a mailbox or utility pole or other structure.
  • Cost to remove debris from the accident

Liability Coverage Does Not Cover At-fault Accidents

If you’re determined to have caused an accident, your liability coverage will not cover you, only the damages and injuries you caused.

You will not get a claim payout from your insurance company if you’re the person at fault, unless you have collision coverage.

You will not get a claim payout if your car is vandalized or stolen unless you have comprehensive coverage.

Collision and comprehensive often are purchased together and, when bundled, are called full coverage.

It only makes sense to have these coverages if your car is worth $4,000 or more. You can lower your monthly premiums on these add-on coverages by increasing your deductible, which is always deducted from a claim payout.

California Car Insurance Limits FAQs

Is uninsured motorist coverage required in California?

No uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage is not required in California, but it’s a good coverage to buy because there are so many uninsured drivers on the road. If they cause an accident, you may be on your own to pay for repairs.

Will my collision coverage pay for repairs with an at-fault uninsured motorist?

No, only uninsured motorists coverage will cover an accident with an uninsured motorist who caused the accident. You are only covered by collision if you caused the accident.

Why did California raise minimum liability limits?

Accidents cost more nowadays, and the last time limits were increased was 58 years ago. Cars cost on average over $40,000 to replace. Repairs are more expensive too due to the technology on newer cars.
