    Is It Mandatory to Buy Homeowners Insurance in Rhode Island?

    You aren’t required to buy homeowners insurance, and it’s not mandatory by law in Rhode Island.

    But most homeowners have a home mortgage; in that case, your lender will likely require a policy to protect its investment.

    The lender will want coverage equal to at least the value of your loan.

    What is Homeowners Insurance?

    Homeowners insurance protects your home and your personal property from the financial impact of unforeseen or unexpected disasters.

    The insurance will pay to have your home repaired or rebuilt after damage from fire, heavy winds, hurricanes, and other natural disasters if your policy covers them.

    In addition, if your personal belongings in your home are stolen or destroyed, homeowners insurance will replace them.

    Homeowners insurance usually covers interior damage, exterior damage, and injury that arises while on the property.

    Every homeowners insurance policy has a liability limit, which determines the amount of coverage the insured has should an unfortunate incident occur.

    What Does a Basic Homeowners Insurance Cover in Rhode Island?

    Dwelling

    This covers damage to your house. So, if your home is destroyed due to a fire or other covered event or peril, your insurer will cover the cost of rebuilding your home.

    For example, a severe thunderstorm uproots a tree that falls onto your home, crushing part of the roof and attic. You’d pay your share of the repair cost — your deductible — and then the insurer would pay the rest up to the limit of your dwelling coverage.

    Medical Payments

    This pays if someone is injured on your property and needs medical treatment, regardless of who’s at fault. It also pays if you, a family member, or a pet like a dog injures someone elsewhere.

    For example, if a guest visits your home and slips in the shower, causing the guest to break her hip, medical payments will provide coverage for the guest’s medical bills.

    Personal Liability

    This pays for medical bills, repair, or replacement costs if you are held responsible for physically hurting someone or damaging his or her personal belongings. Personal liability also handles legal fees if that person sues you.

    For example, if that guest slipped in the shower, broke her hip, and sued you, your personal liability will cover you.

    In addition, if you’re faced with legal fees for accidents for which you or someone else on your policy are at fault, once again, personal liability will cover you.

    But note that personal liability only covers injury to other people, not yourself, and not to people listed on your policy.

    Other Structures

    It covers stand-alone outdoor structures like fences, sheds, detached garages, driveways, pools, or anything not attached to your home.

    For example, if a fire burns down your Shed, your other structures coverage will pay to rebuild it.

    Personal Property

    It covers the cash value to replace your belongings if your house is robbed or burned. Covered personal property often includes furniture, clothing, electronics, everyday items like kitchenware, as well as high-cost valuables like jewelry.

    For example, if your home is burglarized, your personal property coverage will pay to replace any items stolen.

    Loss of Use

    If a covered event destroys your property to the point that it is unhabitable, loss of use will pay for your temporary living expenses.

    For example, if a fire destroys your home, loss of use will pay for dwelling coverage, such as staying in hotels or motels or eating at restaurants. Insurers usually try to find similar accommodations for you to stay and which resemble your damaged home.

    What Events Does a Homeowners Insurance Policy Cover?

    A covered peril is an event your insurer agrees to reimburse you if you file a claim.

    Covered perils include:

    • Fire or lightning.
    • Smoke.
    • Windstorms and hail.
    • Explosions.
    • Theft.
    • Vandalism.
    • Weight of ice, snow, and sleet.
    • Sudden damage from a power surge.
    • Volcanic eruptions.
    • Falling objects.
    • Water overflow or discharge from household systems like plumbing, air conditioning, and appliances.
    • Sudden tearing, cracking, or bulging of hot water, steam, air conditioning, or fire protection system.
    • Riots.
    • Damage from aircraft.
    • Damage caused by vehicles.

    What Factors Determine Your Homeowners Insurance Rate?

    Your rate will be different from another person’s rate due to certain factors that can lower or increase your rate. These include:

    State and ZIP Code

    Your rate will be lower than average if you live in a safe neighborhood that is not prone to constant natural disasters.

    Construction of Home

    How your house was built can affect your premium in a few ways. Some construction types are more resistant to certain types of damage, like wind or fire, which can lower your premium.

    Age of Home

    Newer homes are generally up to code and less likely to experience damage from a variety of causes, such as weather or plumbing issues.

    However, the building materials used in older homes may not conform to current building standards, meaning additional work may be needed to repair or replace them. Expenses to update materials could drive your premium up.

    Distance To Nearest Fire Station

    The closer you are to a fire station, the more likely emergency responders can put a fire out faster than if you live farther away, which could minimize damage.

    Deductible

    Your deductible is the amount you agree to pay out of pocket if you file a claim. If you opt for a higher deductible, the insurer will pay less if you file a claim, which is good.

    Coverage Options

    If you purchase optional coverages to add to your policy, you will likely pay more.

    Credit Score

    In most states, your credit score affects your home insurance premium, as homeowners with lower credit are statistically more likely to file a claim than homeowners with higher credit scores.

    Read more: How to Check Your Credit Score Rating.

    Claim History

    Your rate may skyrocket if you have filed a claim within the last three to five years. Even if you change insurers, your new carrier will be able to see any past claims and may charge you accordingly.

    How Much Does Homeowners Insurance Cost?

    As you can see above, several factors can increase or decrease what you pay in terms of premiums.

    Larger homes will cost more to insure than smaller ones because the former requires more labor and materials to rebuild them.

    A newer home is usually cheaper to insure than an older one because you can expect fewer problems to arise. New homes come with new plumbing and electrical fixtures, as well as new HVAC systems and new appliances.

    This may sound unfair, but your rate will most likely be higher if homeowners near you or in your town have filed a lot of claims over the years.

    A home in an area prone to flooding, like Rhode Island, will cost more to insure due to greater exposure to wind, storms, and erosion.

    Finally, if you live in a home tricked out with security measures such as alarms or security cameras or if you live in a safe area where the crime rate is low, or if you live in a gated community with security guards or keycode entrance, you’ll pay less for your rates.

    How Fast Do Insurance Companies Process Claims?

    Most home insurance providers are able to process and settle claims in a timely manner. In general, the average time frame is within 30 days.

    However, if the claim involves injuries or catastrophic losses like numerous homes in a clustered area getting damaged at the same time due to wildfire spreading, it may take longer to receive a payout on your claim.

    What Will Homeowners Insurance Not Cover?

    A standard homeowner insurance policy never covers flooding or earthquakes.

    Earthquakes

    While earthquake damage is typically excluded, if you live on a fault line or an area susceptible to this natural disaster, it’s a necessary coverage to purchase. An earthquake policy can be expensive, but it’s easy to add as an endorsement. Or you may need to take out a separate policy on top of your basic plan.

    Flooding

    If a storm results in a deluge of flooding and the water comes into your home, homeowners insurance will not pay for any damages.

    In general, if the flood is a result of a natural disaster, your insurer won’t cover you.

    The only time an insurer will pay for flooding is if it is the result of

    water that backs up, overflows, or discharges from sewers, drains, sump pumps, or related equipment.

    In Rhode Island, homeowners who experience flooding due to a natural disaster like a hurricane should purchase a flood insurance policy, although a few homeowners insurance companies offer flood coverage as an endorsement or rider.

    Because Rhode Island is at risk from excessive flooding, residents here pay higher premiums.

    What Are the Types of Homeowners Insurance?

    There are two basic types of homeowners insurance: actual cash value (ACV) and replacement cost value (RCV).

    RCV comes as an add-on, although there are a few companies in which RCV comes as standard.

    The main difference is that RCV will pay you in full while ACV will pay you a depreciated cost.

    Replacement Cost Value

    RCV is about getting full coverage for your belongings and home if destroyed.

    If your personal belongings get damaged, RCV will pay for them in full using today’s prices.

    RCV will also pay the actual cost of rebuilding your home after it is damaged, even if there are increases in labor and building materials, to name a few.

    Actual Cost Value

    For ACV, if your possessions are stolen or destroyed, this insurance will not replace them at their full cost but at their depreciated value.

    Unlike RCV, ACV can leave you short of funds if you need to rebuild your home in the event of a total loss.

    Other options available:

    Extended Replacement Cost Value

    This coverage will pay out more than your dwelling coverage, up to a specified limit, if that’s what it takes to repair your home. This gives you a cushion if rebuilding is more expensive than you expected.

    Guaranteed Replacement Cost Value

    This coverage pays the full cost to repair or replace your home after a covered loss, even if it exceeds your policy limits. Not all insurance companies offer this coverage.

    How Much Insurance Do I Need?

    The amount of home insurance you need depends on your personal property and how valuable it is.

    To determine how much home insurance you should get, you’ll have to do a home inventory of all your personal property.

    The better your documentation, the easier it will be to file a claim should you need to.

    Here are the steps to make the process easy:

    • Go through your entire home and keep a record of all your belongings that you find by taking pictures or filming a video.
    • Estimate what each property costs to file a claim and replace your belongings. To figure out the cost of an item, use receipts. If you don’t have them, find comparable items online.
    • Don’t overlook anything. Say you are wearing expensive clothes. Just because you wear them always doesn’t mean they’re exempt from your inventory.

    Kitchen and bath items like bed sheets, duvets, linens, silverware, and dishware collections should be included, and items should be stored in the attic or garage, such as your bike or that $3,000 faux Christmas tree you bought from Frontgate.

    Be sure to include your expensive items like collectibles, musical instruments, sporting equipment, outdoor furniture, and a barbecue grill.

    • Note when and where you purchased the items, particularly expensive ones.
    • Consider what it would cost to replace each item with an equivalent new item, not what you paid for it initially.

    Replacement costs can change quickly; even a two-year-old appliance might be considerably more expensive today than when you bought it.

    • Once you've thoroughly inventory your belongings, you'll know how much homeowners insurance you need.
    • Depending on the value of your belongings, you can opt for higher coverage limits for various parts of your policy.

    For most homeowners, this usually includes high-cost valuables, such as jewelry and pricey artwork, that your standard policy can’t cover in full.

    Do I Need Extra Coverage for My Valuables?

    The personal property coverage in a standard homeowners policy pays up to a certain limit for damaged or stolen belongings.

    So, if you own high-value items, like jewelry, fine art, or musical instruments, the coverage provided in your basic policy may not be enough to replace them if you have to file a claim.

    For example, if your engagement ring is stolen, there may be a cap of $500 or $1,000 or more to replace it, depending on the insurer, and you could be subject to your policy’s deductible.

    To make sure your expensive jewelry is insured to its full value, you should schedule it.

    Scheduling your personal property means you’ll list it for its full value. So, if you schedule your engagement ring, for example, it will be removed from your personal property limits and insured separately.

    How Can I Lower My Homeowners Insurance Premium?

    The three most popular ways are to get quotes from different insurers, increase your credit rating, and apply for discounts.

    Shop Around

    Get quotes from as many insurers as you can, although those in the industry suggest three quotes from three different insurers is sufficient.

    A quote is an estimate of your premium cost. If you compare multiple quotes for the same coverage from different companies, you may likely find the best insurer for you.

    You can get home insurance quotes online or over the phone.

    Increase Your Credit Score

    Maintaining a good credit rating can significantly reduce your rates.

    Some ways that should improve your credit-based insurance score include:

    • Order your full credit report, check your report for mistakes, and dispute them.
    • Pay bills on time.
    • Pay more than the minimum due on your credit cards.
    • Pay off a credit card in full.
    • Forego hard inquiries so your credit score doesn’t drop.
    • Use credit responsibly.

    Your premiums likely won’t reduce overnight, but if you can improve and maintain a higher credit rating, your credit-based insurance score might also improve, which could lower your rates.

    Discounts

    During the quote process, ask each company if any discounts apply to you. Taking advantage of home insurance discounts is one of the easiest ways to lower your premium.

    The most important discount is the multi-policy discount, which takes a good percentage off your premium. So, if you bundle home with auto, you’ll see your rates drop.

    Other discounts include making your home safe, getting new plumbing, HVAC, and other electrical systems, as well as purchasing a new roof made from sturdier materials to make it less vulnerable to wind damage or the weight of heavy snow.

    How to Buy Homeowners Insurance

    Coverage

    Determine how much coverage you need.  Once you find a company you like, research it thoroughly to see what coverages it offers, what it doesn’t have, and what they need.

    Make a Fair Comparison

    When evaluating rates, ensure each insurer offers the same limits and deductibles.

    Check Consumer Complaints

    Use vetted customer reviews from insurance review sites to see how many consumer complaints each insurer has before you choose a policy.

    Website and Mobile App

    Choose a company that has a useful and easy-to-use website and mobile app. If you can take care of most of your insurance needs online, from buying a policy to submitting and tracking a claim, you can save time and don’t have to be on hold while attempting to contact an agent.
