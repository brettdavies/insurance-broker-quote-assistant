If you’ve just returned to the U.S. after living abroad, getting your [car insurance](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/) set up might not be as simple as picking up where you left off. Even if you carried insurance overseas, many American [insurers](https://www.thezebra.com/insurance-companies/best-car-insurance-companies/) will view that time as a gap in coverage unless you can show [proof of insurance](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/insurance-guide/proof-of-insurance/) (in English).

Not all insurance companies will approve someone [without continuous coverage](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/policies/car-insurance-canceled-policies/). Because of the risks presented by an uninsured driver, the insurance company might underwrite your car insurance policy with a subsidiary.
When returning to the States from abroad, be sure to secure proof of your expat car insurance. This record will help to prove your continuously insured status. If it looks like your [coverage has lapsed](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/coverage/car-insurance-after-lapsed-coverage/), some insurers might not cover you at all or quote you higher premiums. No matter your unique circumstance, finding [affordable car insurance](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/how-to-shop/best-cheap-auto-insurance/) for expats isn't impossible.
