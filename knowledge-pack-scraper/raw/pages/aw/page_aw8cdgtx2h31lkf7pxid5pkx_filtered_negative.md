# Liability vs Full Coverage Car Insurance for Chicago Drivers
UPDATED: October 13, 2025
Share:
[ ](https://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.insureonthespot.com%2Fliability-vs-full-coverage-car-insurance-chicago%2F) [ ](https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=Liability+vs+Full+Coverage+Car+Insurance+for+Chicago+Drivers&url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.insureonthespot.com%2Fliability-vs-full-coverage-car-insurance-chicago%2F)
**Liability vs. full coverage car insurance** is a common dilemma for drivers in Chicago and across Illinois. The choice comes down to how much protection you want for your vehicle versus just meeting legal requirements. Illinois law mandates **liability car insurance** as the minimum coverage – this pays for injuries or damage you cause to others. **Full coverage car insurance** isn’t a single type of policy but rather means you have both liability and added coverages (collision and comprehensive) to protect your car as well.

## **What is the Difference Between Liability and Full Coverage Car Insurance in Illinois?**
Illinois law requires every driver to carry liability insurance at a minimum. **Liability coverage** helps pay for the other party’s medical bills or repair costs if you’re at fault in an accident. It is often referred to as “state-minimum” coverage – in Illinois this minimum is **25/50/20** (that means $25,000 bodily injury per person, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $20,000 property damage per accident).
However, liability insurance **does not cover your own car** or your injuries. That’s where additional coverages like collision and comprehensive come in. **Full coverage** generally means you have liability insurance **plus** these coverages to protect your vehicle. **Collision coverage** pays for damage to your car from crashes (for example, if you hit another vehicle or a fixed object, or have a single-car accident).
**Comprehensive coverage** covers non-collision incidents like theft, vandalism, fire, or hail damage to your car. If you have an auto loan or lease, your lender will typically require you to carry collision and comprehensive – essentially “full coverage” – to protect their interest in the vehicle.
In summary, here’s how they compare at a high level:
* **Liability Only:** Covers injuries and property damage **to others** when you are at fault. It’s required by law (Illinois’ minimum **25/50/20** limits). This coverage does **not** pay for any damage to your own car or your medical bills.
* **Full Coverage:** Refers to a policy including liability **plus** coverage for your own vehicle (through collision and comprehensive insurance). It covers damage to your car from accidents you cause, as well as non-accident threats like theft or weather. Full coverage is often required if your car is financed, and it provides much broader protection for you as a driver.
## **How Does Choosing Liability vs Full Coverage Affect Chicago Insurance Rates?**
Opting for full coverage will **come at a higher price** than carrying liability only. Adding collision and comprehensive to your policy can roughly **double** your insurance premium compared to the cost of minimum liability coverage. This is because you’re insuring your own vehicle against a wide range of risks. Chicago drivers also typically pay more for car insurance overall than drivers in less populated areas. That means full coverage in the city can cost **substantially more** in premiums than a basic liability-only policy.
Why are Chicago rates higher, especially for full coverage? Here are a few key factors:
* **Heavy traffic & accidents:** Chicago’s dense urban traffic means a higher chance of collisions and claims. With congested highways and busy city streets, the likelihood of accidents (and insurance payouts) goes up, which raises rates.
* **Theft & vandalism:** Big-city living comes with higher crime risks. The city had over **29,000** vehicle thefts in 2023 – a record high – leading to more comprehensive claims. Cars in Chicago are simply at greater risk of theft or vandalism than in smaller towns, and insurers factor that into full coverage costs.
* **Urban costs:** Repair costs and medical claim expenses tend to be higher in major cities. From higher labor rates at Chicago auto shops to more frequent injury claims, insuring a vehicle in the city is pricier. These costs drive up premiums for all coverage types.
As a result of these factors, a liability-only policy is much cheaper than full coverage for most Chicago drivers. However, it’s important to remember that with liability-only, **any damage to your own car would come out of your pocket**. You’re essentially trading lower upfront cost for more financial risk if an accident or theft happens.
## **Should Chicago Drivers Get Liability-Only or Full Coverage Car Insurance?**
Deciding between liability-only and full coverage comes down to your personal situation. Key factors include your car’s value, your budget, your loan/lease status, and your comfort with risk. There is no one-size-fits-all answer – it’s about balancing the cost of insurance versus the protection you gain.
In Chicago, you should also weigh local risks when making your decision. A dense urban environment means higher chances of accidents, plus a greater risk of theft or vandalism. These local factors might make full coverage more worthwhile for many city drivers, even though it costs more. On the other hand, if your vehicle is older or not worth much, you may decide that paying extra for collision and comprehensive coverage isn’t financially sensible. Below are some guidelines to help you choose:
**When Liability-Only May Be Enough:**
* You drive an older or low-value car that’s fully paid off (for example, a car worth only a few thousand dollars).
* You have enough savings to repair or replace your vehicle out-of-pocket if it gets damaged or totaled.
* You want the lowest possible insurance premium and are willing to accept the risk of no coverage for your own car.
**When Full Coverage Is Recommended:**
* Your car is newer, higher-value, or financed/leased – lenders will **require** collision and comprehensive coverage in these cases.
* You live or park in a high-risk area of Chicago (high rates of accidents or auto theft) and want protection against those hazards.
* You couldn’t easily afford to replace your car if it were stolen or wrecked. In this case, paying extra for full coverage gives you peace of mind that an insurance payout would cover major repairs or a replacement vehicle.
By considering these factors, Chicago drivers can make an informed choice. Always remember that carrying at least the Illinois state minimum liability coverage is the law – beyond that, it’s about what level of protection makes sense for **you**. If you’re unsure, an insurance agent can help you weigh the cost difference and the potential risks of going with liability-only versus full coverage.
## **Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)**
* * *
### **Q: What’s the minimum auto insurance required in Illinois?**
**A:** Illinois requires at least **25/50/20** liability coverage on every auto policy. That means $25,000 of bodily injury coverage per person, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury to others, and $20,000 for property damage. This is the legal state minimum to drive, though many drivers opt for higher limits (and additional coverages) for better protection.
* * *
### **Q: If I have a car loan, do I need full coverage in Illinois?**
**A:** Yes. While Illinois law itself doesn’t mandate full coverage, most lenders will require you to carry collision and comprehensive coverage if your car is financed or leased. This “full coverage” requirement is part of your loan/lease agreement to protect the vehicle. Once your car is paid off, full coverage becomes optional – but it may still be wise depending on your car’s value.
* * *
### **Q: Is car insurance more expensive in Chicago than in other areas?**
**A:** Generally, yes. Chicago drivers tend to pay higher premiums than those in smaller Illinois towns or rural areas. The city’s heavy traffic and higher accident frequency, combined with more car thefts and vandalism, make insurance riskier and therefore more costly. Both liability and full coverage rates are typically elevated in Chicago to account for these factors.
* * *
### **Q: Will liability insurance pay for damages to my own car?**
**A:** No – liability coverage only pays for damage or injuries **to others** when you are at fault in an accident. It will not pay for your own vehicle’s repairs or your medical bills. To cover damage to your car, you would need collision coverage (to cover crash-related damage) and comprehensive coverage (to cover theft, fire, or other non-collision damage), which are included when you have full coverage.
* * *
### **Q: Should I drop full coverage on an older car?**
**A:** If your car is older and has a low market value, you might consider dropping collision and comprehensive coverage to save on premiums. As a rule of thumb, if the annual cost of full coverage approaches 10% of your car’s value, it may not be cost-effective. Just be sure you’re comfortable paying for any repairs or even replacing the car out of pocket if you carry only liability on that vehicle.
