* [Six months after renewing](https://www.aol.com/finance/best-time-to-shop-for-car-insurance-181059919.html) your current policy

## 3. Consider a pay-per-mile program

Your retirement lifestyle might mean less time on the road, and that could translate into significant savings on your car insurance. ‚ÄúIf you're driving relatively infrequently ‚Äî less than 8,000 to 10,000 miles annually ‚Äî it may be worth exploring a pay-per-mile program,‚Äù says Maya Afilalo, a car insurance expert and industry analyst.

## 4. Enroll in use-based programs

Usage-based insurance (UBI) programs have gained popularity, especially among retirees who no longer commute to work. However, these programs may not be right for everyone. Tracking devices are known to misinterpret defensive driving techniques as risky behavior. And you‚Äôll want to consider privacy, as these programs are designed to collect real-time data about your driving habits and movements.

## 6. Don‚Äôt file unnecessary claims

This is a big one, because nothing will jack up your premium faster than filing a damage claim.

Experts say it best to avoid filing claims that are under $1,000 to $2,000, because you'll likely end up paying more in increased premiums over time than you'd receive from the claim payout.

## 8. Keep a healthy credit score

Having good to excellent credit can unlock more than lower rates on [personal loans](https://www.aol.com/finance/personal-loan-vs-home-equity-loan-015311390.html) and [premium credit card rewards](https://www.aol.com/finance/credit-card-rewards-travel-182136384.html). Drivers with poor credit pay 76% more for full coverage than drivers with good credit, [according to a recent report](#sources), with even higher rates that depend on where you live. Put another way, having a poor credit score can lead to paying double or more for your car insurance ‚Äî highlighting the need to maintain a healthy credit score, [even in retirement](https://www.aol.com/finance/how-retirement-affects-credit-score-221528026.html).

Whether fair or not, many auto insurers use [credit-based insurance scores](https://www.iii.org/article/background-on-insurance-scoring) as a way to predict whether you‚Äôre more or less likely to submit a claim against your policy. And the rise in artificial intelligence models could mean we‚Äôll see [real-time premium adjustments](https://www.insurancethoughtleadership.com/ai-machine-learning/how-ai-could-set-premiums-real-time) linked to your credit score.

## 9. Maintain a clean driving record

A driving record free of violations, citations and accidents is ultimately the best way to keep your premiums low. Your motor vehicle record is one of the most important factors that auto insurers use to determine the rates you‚Äôll pay for car insurance coverage. And while a speeding ticket can stick around on your motor vehicle record for up to three years, a DUI or DWI can linger for five years or longer, [limiting your coverage options](https://www.aol.com/finance/situations-standard-car-insurance-wont-cover-151412189.html) and driving up what you‚Äôll pay ‚Äî from $10 a month for minor infractions to more than $800 a month for more severe violations, [according to AAA](https://cluballiance.aaa.com/the-extra-mile/advice/budget/how-much-does-car-insurance-increase-after-accident).

Interested in seeing what insurers are reviewing? Contact your local DMV to request a copy of your driving record. You might be surprised what‚Äôs in it.

## Can an insurer refuse to issue or renew a policy based on age?

No. The Age Discrimination Act provides important protections for seniors, which means, by extension, that insurance companies can‚Äôt refuse coverage based solely on age. However, they can consider factors that often correlate with age, such as medical conditions and driving history.

"For example, if your doctor notifies the DMV that you're unfit to drive, your license could be suspended, and your carrier could drop you, leading to higher premiums down the line," says Afilalo. ‚ÄúHowever, minor health issues ‚Äî like needing to wear glasses or contact lenses while driving ‚Äî won't affect your insurance rates.‚Äù

Some state DMVs can require additional testing for specific age groups, such as California's in-person renewal tests for drivers older than 70. Contact [your state's motor vehicle services](https://www.usa.gov/state-motor-vehicle-services) for details on these programs and general DMV services.

## FAQs: Mature drivers and auto insurance coverage

Learn more about how auto insurance quotes, policies and rates work with these common questions. And take a look at [our growing library of personal finance guides](https://www.aol.com/business/personal-finance/) that can help you save money, earn money and grow your wealth.

### I‚Äôm moving to a new state. How do I transfer my auto insurance policy?

When you move to a new state, you can‚Äôt always transfer your existing auto insurance policy. Each state requires different insurance regulations and minimum coverage levels. You'll likely need to get a new policy or update your current policy so that complies with your new state's laws. Get in touch with [your local DMV for guidance](https://www.usa.gov/state-motor-vehicle-services).

### Will shopping for quotes hurt my credit score?

No. Insurance companies will conduct a ‚Äúsoft pull‚Äù of your credit for quote inquiries, which doesn‚Äôt affect your credit score. We break it all down in [our guide to the surprising factors that can damage your credit](https://www.aol.com/finance/what-hurts-credit-score-180352098.html) ‚Äî including what you *don't* need to worry about.

### Will my car insurance policy cover guests or temporary drivers?

Yes. Your car insurance typically covers most loved ones who borrow your car occasionally during holiday visits, thanks to what insurance companies call "permissive use." This means if you give someone permission to drive your car, your policy usually extends coverage to them. However, important limitations and considerations exist, especially for longer stays or frequent borrowing. Learn more in our guide to [auto insurance coverage when lending your car](https://www.aol.com/finance/auto-insurance-lending-car-153114885.html).

### My auto insurance went up. Is this age discrimination?

No, a rate increase in auto insurance isn't necessarily age discrimination. That said, insurance companies can legally use age as one of many factors in setting rates, as long as they base it on statistical data about accident risks for different age groups ‚Äî and not your specific age only.

### Does being an AARP member decrease my car insurance premiums?

Not necessarily. While AARP partners with The Hartford to offer auto insurance to its members, you'll still want to compare rates with other insurers ‚Äî you might find a better deal by shopping around. Learn more about benefits in [our guide to AARP membership (and whether it's worth the dues)](https://www.aol.com/finance/aarp-worth-it-201257670.html).

### At what point is car insurance not worth it?

If your car is more than 10 years old and worth less than $3,000, you might consider dropping comprehensive and collision coverage while maintaining the required liability insurance. However, your decision should be based on your state‚Äôs requirements for minimum coverage and your personal situation.
