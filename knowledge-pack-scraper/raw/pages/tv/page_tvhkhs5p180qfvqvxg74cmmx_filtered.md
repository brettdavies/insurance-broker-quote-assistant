# Florida Hurricane Shutter Law: What Homeowners Need to Know

Understand Florida's hurricane shutter regulations, including state codes, local rules, HOA guidelines, and insurance requirements for homeowners.

Hurricane shutters play a crucial role in protecting homes from storm damage, especially in Florida, where hurricanes are a recurring threat. Homeowners must understand the legal requirements to ensure compliance and safeguard their properties effectively.

## Florida Building Code Requirements

The Florida Building Code (FBC) establishes minimum construction standards, including hurricane shutter requirements. Homes in wind-borne debris regions—primarily coastal and high-risk areas—must have impact-resistant windows or approved storm shutters. The code references ASTM E1886 and ASTM E1996, which set impact resistance standards to ensure shutters can withstand debris propelled by hurricane-force winds.

In high-velocity hurricane zones, including Miami-Dade and Broward counties, stricter standards apply. Homes must use shutters or impact-resistant glazing that meets Miami-Dade County’s Notice of Acceptance (NOA) standards. Homeowners replacing windows or doors in these areas must also comply to maintain structural integrity.

Shutters must be securely fastened to withstand wind pressures based on location and design. Proper anchoring methods prevent shutters from becoming hazardous projectiles. Additionally, shutters must be operable without specialized tools, ensuring quick deployment before a storm.

## Local Ordinances

Florida municipalities and counties can enact additional regulations on hurricane shutters. Some coastal cities require installation by licensed contractors and periodic inspections to ensure functionality. Local governments may also require permits, with fees varying by jurisdiction.

Many cities regulate how long shutters can remain in place outside hurricane season. Fort Lauderdale and Naples, for example, require removal within 15 to 30 days after a storm threat to address fire safety and aesthetic concerns. Non-compliance can result in citations or removal orders.

Historic preservation districts may impose further restrictions. Homes in these areas often need approval for shutters that align with architectural styles, requiring additional permitting and public hearings.

## Homeowners Association Rules

Homeowners associations (HOAs) regulate shutter installation and use within their communities. Florida law prevents HOAs from prohibiting hurricane shutters but allows them to enforce design, color, and installation requirements. These rules maintain neighborhood uniformity and are outlined in the community’s Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs).

Homeowners typically must seek HOA approval before installation, submitting plans specifying type and placement. Some associations mandate specific brands or models, while others restrict permanent shutters in favor of removable panels. Non-compliance can lead to disputes or denied installation requests.

HOAs may also regulate when shutters can be deployed, prohibiting prolonged use outside hurricane season. Homeowners may need to remove or retract shutters within a set timeframe after a storm threat, with violations leading to fines or legal action.

## Rental Property Mandates

Florida landlords must comply with legal obligations regarding hurricane shutters. While state law does not explicitly require installation, the Florida Residential Landlord and Tenant Act mandates that rental units meet building, housing, and health codes. If local regulations or lease agreements require storm protection, landlords must ensure shutters are installed and functional before hurricane season.

Lease agreements determine responsibility for shutter deployment. Some leases assign this duty to landlords, while others place it on tenants. Courts generally rule that landlords cannot require tenants to install shutters if it creates an undue burden, particularly in multi-unit buildings where access to upper floors is limited.

## Insurance Requirements

Homeowners insurance policies in Florida often include provisions related to hurricane shutters, affecting coverage eligibility and premiums. Under state law, insurers must offer premium reductions for homes with approved windstorm mitigation features, including shutters that meet impact standards. Permanent shutters typically qualify for greater savings than temporary panels.

Failure to install shutters in high-risk areas can result in higher premiums or policy exclusions for wind damage. Some insurers require proof of compliance through wind mitigation inspections. If shutters are installed but not used during a storm, insurers may deny claims for wind-driven debris damage. Policyholders should review their agreements to ensure they meet coverage requirements.

## Penalties and Enforcement

Local governments and HOAs enforce shutter regulations through fines, code enforcement actions, and legal remedies. Municipal code officers can issue citations for violations such as failing to remove shutters within the required timeframe or installing unapproved models. Fines range from $100 to $500 per violation, with additional daily penalties for non-compliance. Repeated infractions may lead to property liens and potential foreclosure.

HOAs can impose fines of up to $100 per day for non-compliance, up to a $1,000 maximum unless governing documents allow higher penalties. Persistent violations may lead to legal action, with courts weighing homeowners’ rights against HOA regulations. Some disputes escalate to arbitration or litigation, particularly when aesthetic and safety concerns conflict with storm protection needs.
