What is the minimum auto insurance coverage in New York?

New York requires drivers to have a minimum amount of bodily injury and property damage liability coverage, sometimes written as 25/50/10. In addition, drivers in New York must have uninsured motorist bodily injury coverage and personal injury protection.

  • Bodily injury liability (BI): $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident, plus double for death
  • Property damage liability (PD): $10,000 per accident
  • Uninsured motorist bodily injury (UMBI): $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident, plus double for death
  • Personal injury protection (PIP): $50,000 per accident

Supplemental spousal liability insurance NY

All auto insurance policies for married drivers in New York automatically include a new coverage called supplemental spousal liability insurance (SSLI), as of August 2023.

This coverage allows you to make a claim against your spouse's insurance if you're hurt in an accident while they're driving.

Your supplemental spousal liability insurance limit is typically the same as your bodily injury liability limit. However, you can only use this coverage once you've used all of your personal injury protection (PIP) coverage.

You can decline supplemental spousal liability insurance by contacting your insurance company and filling out a form stating you're turning down this coverage.

Supplemental spousal liability insurance costs an average of $22 per year.

However the cost varies by company. For example, Allstate doesn't charge extra for this coverage. But Geico customers pay an additional $73 per year for the coverage, on average.

What's the best car insurance coverage to get in New York?

Most New York drivers should get full coverage car insurance, which includes comprehensive and collision coverage.

If you have a car loan or lease, your lender typically requires you to have these coverages. That's because they pay for damage to your car in many situations, regardless of whose fault it is. You should also consider adding comprehensive and collision coverage if your car is less than eight years old or worth more than $5,000.

In addition, full coverage policies typically have higher liability limits than the state requirement. Higher liability limits are a good idea because New York's required limits may not be high enough to pay for a serious accident.

For example, New York only required $10,000 of property damage liability insurance. If you crash into a Tesla and the other car is totaled, $10,000 won't be enough to fully replace the other driver's car.

The average cost of auto insurance in New York is $189 per month for a full coverage policy. That's $14 per month more than the national average. This is partially because of the heavy traffic in highly populated areas like New York City and Long Island, which means a greater risk of accidents.

Why is car insurance so expensive in New York?

New York is the ninth-most expensive state for minimum-coverage insurance, and the 16th most expensive for full coverage. This is in part because New York is a no-fault state, which means drivers need to have personal injury protection. This coverage can be expensive. In addition, many areas in New York have heavy traffic congestion, which can lead to more accidents.

Methodology

To find the best cheap auto insurance in New York state, ValuePenguin collected quotes from the largest NY auto insurance companies across all ZIP codes in the state. Quotes are for a 30-year-old man with good credit who owns a 2015 Honda Civic EX.

Rates are for a full coverage policy with comprehensive and collision coverage, along with higher liability limits than required for New York drivers. Rates don't include supplemental spousal liability coverage.

  • $50,000 of bodily injury liability coverage per person and $100,000 per accident
  • $25,000 of property damage liability
  • $50,000 of bodily injury uninsured motorist coverage per person and $100,000 per accident
  • $50,000 of personal injury protection
  • Collision and comprehensive coverage with a $500 deductible

ValuePenguin's analysis used insurance rate data from Quadrant Information Services. These rates were publicly sourced from insurance company filings and should be used for comparative purposes only. Your own quotes may be different.
