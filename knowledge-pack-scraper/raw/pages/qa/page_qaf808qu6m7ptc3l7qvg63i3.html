How to Get Cheap Car Insurance Premiums After an Accident

An accident increases your car insurance premiums 24% on average, adding $208 annually. Offset this increase by switching providers, comparing quotes regularly, bundling policies, maximizing discounts and adjusting coverage options like deductibles or accident forgiveness.

    Switch car insurance companies

    Your insurer may raise your rates at renewal, but competitors price accidents differently. Switch car insurance after an accident anytime; you don't need to wait for renewal. Work with your previous insurer if you filed a claim to ensure smooth transition.

    Shop around with multiple providers

    Get quotes from at least three providers every six months. Insurance companies use different pricing models, so rates vary widely. Accidents affect your rates less over time, and insurers regularly adjust pricing, so shopping around helps you capture better deals.

    Bundle your home and car insurance policies

    Home and auto bundles result in lower rates. Insuring multiple vehicles with the same company also qualifies you for discounts. Ask about bundling options for potential savings.

    Seek out other discounts

    Accidents don't disqualify you from other discounts. Save on premiums by paying your policy in full, installing safety or security devices or participating in telematics programs like Progressive's Snapshot.

    Switch to a less expensive vehicle

    Your vehicle type affects insurance costs. Cars with better safety features or lower repair costs cost less to insure. Vehicles like the Toyota Camry, known for safety and affordability, help lower premiums.

    Compare Quotes Regularly

    Compare quotes every six months as rates change frequently. Insurers adjust their pricing models regularly, and you may find better deals by shopping around.

    Consider Higher Deductibles

    Increasing your deductible from $500 to $1,000 lowers your premium by 10% to 15%. Deductibles don't apply to liability coverage, which pays for damage you cause to others, only to comprehensive and collision coverage for your vehicle.

    Ask About Accident Forgiveness

    Ask about accident forgiveness before another accident occurs. Adding this coverage now protects you from rate increases if you have a future accident.

    Maintain Continuous Coverage

    Avoid gaps in coverage to prevent additional penalties. Coverage gaps result in higher rates when you restart your policy because insurers view lapses as increased risk.

    Complete Defensive Driving Courses

    State-approved defensive driving programs qualify you for 5% to 10% discounts with many insurers. Check with your provider about eligible courses in your state.

What Makes Car Insurance Cheaper After an Accident?

Insurance companies evaluate multiple factors when setting premiums for drivers with accidents. Understanding these factors helps you target the right insurers and coverage options for the most affordable rates.

Insurance Company Risk Assessment Policies

Insurers use different algorithms to calculate risk and set rates after accidents. Some companies are more forgiving than others:

  • State Farm: Often offers the lowest rates for single-accident drivers
  • Travelers: Includes accident forgiveness in many policies automatically
  • Progressive: Uses Snapshot technology to focus on current driving behavior rather than past accidents
  • GEICO: Provides substantial military and federal employee discounts that offset accident penalties
  • Auto-Owners: Regional focus allows for more personalized risk assessment

Time Since Accident

Most insurers reduce penalties after three years, with full rate restoration after five years. Insurance companies view recent accidents (within 12 months) as the highest risk, with penalties decreasing gradually. After three years, many insurers treat you like drivers with clean records.

Accident Severity and Fault Status

Minor fender-benders with no injuries result in smaller rate increases than accidents involving injuries or major property damage. At-fault accidents carry higher penalties than not-at-fault incidents. Total payout amount matters: a $500 fender-bender affects your rates less than a $15,000 accident with medical claims.

Your Overall Driving Record

Insurers review your driving history over three to five years, including speeding tickets, DUIs and other violations. Drivers with one accident but no other infractions qualify for standard rates, while those with multiple incidents have higher premiums.

Geographic Location Impact

Urban areas with higher accident frequencies may impose smaller penalties compared to rural areas. Some states limit how much insurers can increase rates after accidents, while others allow more pricing flexibility.

Credit Score and Financial Stability

In states where allowed, insurers consider your credit score when setting rates after an accident. Drivers with excellent credit receive smaller rate increases than those with poor credit.

Factors That Raise Insurance Rates After an Accident

Your insurance premium increases depend on several factors beyond just having an accident. Some factors like your address and credit score are within your control, while others such as age and local insurance costs are not.

    State Laws

    Car insurance rate increases vary by state after an at-fault accident. State laws regulate how much insurers can raise rates to ensure reasonable increases. Understanding these guidelines helps you anticipate changes in costs.

    Age

    Age affects rate changes after an accident. Rates are lowest from your early 20s through your 60s. Younger drivers face higher rates due to inexperience, while older drivers may see increases because of slower reaction times.

    Fault Status

    At-fault accidents increase your rates more than not-at-fault accidents. Some insurers offer accident forgiveness that waives the first at-fault incident.

    Insurance Provider

    Each company has different policies for rate increases after accidents. Some insurers are more forgiving than others when calculating your new premium.

    Other Infractions

    Multiple violations like speeding tickets or DUIs combined with an accident compound your rate increase.

    Injuries or Severity of the Accident

    Accidents involving injuries or major property damage result in larger insurance payouts and higher rate increases than minor fender-benders.

How to File an Insurance Car Accident Claim: Step-by-Step Guide

Filing an insurance car accident claim properly affects your settlement amount and processing time. Avoid common mistakes like delayed reporting, accepting low initial offers, admitting fault or inadequate documentation. Follow this process to protect your financial interests and get the best possible outcome.

  1. 1
    Secure the Accident Scene

    Move vehicles out of traffic if possible. Turn on hazard lights, set up flares or reflective triangles and check for injuries. Call 911 if anyone is hurt or if there's major property damage.

  2. 2
    Document Everything Thoroughly
    • Photograph all vehicles from multiple angles, showing damage
    • Capture license plates, VINs and driver's licenses
    • Document the accident scene, including traffic signals, road signs and weather conditions
    • Get contact and insurance information from all drivers involved
    • Obtain contact information from witnesses
    • Record the exact time, location and circumstances of the accident
  3. 3
    Contact the Police and File a Report

    Call the police immediately, even for minor accidents. Many states require police reports for insurance claims.

  4. 4
    Notify Your Insurance Company

    Contact your insurance company within 24 hours to report the accident.

  5. 5
    Seek Medical Attention

    See a doctor within 24 to 48 hours of the accident, even if you feel fine. Some injuries like whiplash or concussions may not show symptoms immediately.

  6. 6
    Work with Your Insurance Adjuster

    Your assigned adjuster will contact you to schedule a vehicle inspection and discuss the claim process.

  7. 7
    Get Repair Estimates

    Obtain repair estimates from at least two reputable auto body shops. Your insurance company may recommend preferred providers.

  8. 8
    Review and Negotiate Settlement

    Carefully review your insurance company's settlement offer.

  9. 9
    Finalize Your Claim

    Once you accept a settlement offer, review all paperwork carefully before signing.

WHEN SHOULD I SEEK LEGAL HELP AFTER AN ACCIDENT?

Consider consulting an attorney when injuries are severe or long-term, fault is disputed between insurance companies, settlement offers seem unreasonably low compared to your damages, or the other driver was uninsured or underinsured. Legal representation becomes valuable if your claim is denied or delayed without a reasonable explanation, you're pressured to settle quickly or multiple parties are involved, requiring a complex liability determination.

How Long Do Accidents Stay on Your Record?

A car accident stays on your record for three to five years, but this varies depending on where you live, the accident's severity and any other infractions involved. An at-fault accident may be removed from your record if no major charges like DUI or reckless driving were added. Most insurers base premiums on your driving history for the past three to five years, which is why accident forgiveness is valuable for drivers seeking insurance after their first accident.

What Happens if You Get Into a Car Accident Without Insurance?

Getting into a car accident without insurance triggers immediate legal and financial consequences that can affect you for years. Here's what you'll face across different areas:

Immediate Legal Consequences

Most states immediately suspend your driver's license after a car accident without insurance. Suspension periods range between 30 days and one year, depending on your state's laws and the accident's severity.

State-Specific Penalties for Car Accidents With No Insurance

Driving without insurance carries severe penalties in both no-fault and at-fault states, including immediate license suspension and mandatory SR-22 filing requirements. At-fault states impose additional consequences like vehicle impoundment and direct civil lawsuit exposure when drivers cause car accidents. While each state establishes minimum insurance coverage requirements, these state minimums provide inadequate financial protection during serious accidents, leaving uninsured drivers vulnerable to out-of-pocket expenses.

Financial Responsibility Requirements

After a car accident without insurance, drivers must demonstrate financial responsibility to regain driving privileges. This process requires:

  • SR-22 certificate filing costing $25 to $50 annually for three to five years
  • Complete payment of outstanding claims and damages
  • Proof of future insurance coverage meeting state minimum requirements

Violations carry substantial financial penalties: first offenses range from $500 to $2,500 depending on state laws, repeat offenses escalate to $1,000 to $5,000 plus potential jail time, and additional court costs and administrative fees add $200 to $500 to total expenses.

Financial Liability When You Have a Car Accident Without Insurance

Without insurance coverage after a car accident, drivers become personally liable for extensive financial damages, including all property damage to other vehicles and property, medical expenses for injured parties that can reach $100,000+ per person, legal fees if sued by other parties, lost wages for injured parties, and pain and suffering damages awarded by courts.

Future Insurance Consequences

A car accident without insurance creates long-term consequences for future coverage affordability. Drivers become classified as "high-risk" requiring expensive SR-22 policies.
