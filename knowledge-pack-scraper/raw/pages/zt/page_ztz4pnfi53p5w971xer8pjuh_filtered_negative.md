Furthermore, all motorists must carry [proof of coverage](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/insurance-guide/proof-of-insurance/) while driving. Those who drive without proper insurance coverage are left at risk and could face steep penalties. If you’d like to learn more about car insurance options and required coverages in [California](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/california-car-insurance/), consult our guide.
These limits are only those mandated by the state of California. If you finance or lease your vehicle, you could face additional requirements from a [lender](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/vehicles/car-insurance-financed-vehicle/) or [leasing](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/vehicles/car-insurance-leased-vehicle/) company. This typically takes the form of physical damage coverage, typically referred to as [full coverage](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/coverage/best-full-coverage-auto-insurance/).
* * *
California requires all drivers to carry **[liability coverage](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/coverage/liability-insurance/)**. This coverage pays for [bodily injuries](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/insurance-guide/bodily-injury-liability/) and [property damage](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/insurance-guide/property-damage-liability/) you cause in an at-fault accident. Liability will also go toward paying an injured party’s lost wages or pain and suffering. It’s important to note that your own liability coverage never pays for your injuries or damages to your own property.
## What are California's state-mandated car insurance  _limits_?
The total amount that your [car insurance company](https://www.thezebra.com/insurance-companies/best-car-insurance-companies/) will pay is determined by the limits that you set. A limit is simply the maximum amount that an insurer will pay out for a claim. Every state determines its own liability coverage limits, though they are almost universally split into the following three categories: **bodily injury per person**, **bodily injury per accident**, and **property damage**. In California, that minimum is listed as 30/60/15[[1]](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/california-car-insurance/state-laws-ca/#footnote-1) and explained below:
* $30,000 in **bodily injury coverage per person** is the maximum amount your insurer will pay for a single person injured by you in an auto accident.
* $60,000 in **bodily injury coverage per accident** is the maximum dollar amount that will be paid for all injuries that you cause in an accident in which more than one person is hurt.
* $15,000 in **property damage per accident** is the highest amount that an insurer will pay for damage that you cause in an accident.
* * *
Those found guilty of [driving with no insurance](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/policies/consequences-of-driving-without-insurance/) in California can expect the following penalties to apply:[[2]](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/california-car-insurance/state-laws-ca/#footnote-2)
* Possible suspension of the vehicle’s registration or your license leading to reinstatement fees ($14 for vehicle registration[[3]](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/california-car-insurance/state-laws-ca/#footnote-3), $125 for your license[[4]](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/california-car-insurance/state-laws-ca/#footnote-4))
* Possible filing of an SR-22 certificate

* **[Comprehensive](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/insurance-guide/comprehensive-insurance/)**: A coverage that pays for damage to your vehicle caused in non-collision circumstances like [theft](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/coverage/does-car-insurance-cover-theft/) or damage related to [weather](https://www.thezebra.com/auto-insurance/coverage/natural-disasters/does-car-insurance-cover-hail-damage/) events.
