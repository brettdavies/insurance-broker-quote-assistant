Florida home insurance coverage: 7 Essential 2025 Tips

Florida Home Insurance Coverage Tips | Schneider & Associates

Understanding Florida’s Unique Insurance Landscape

Florida home insurance coverage provides essential protection for your most valuable asset in a state with extraordinary weather risks. Here’s what you need to know:

  • Standard Coverage: Typically includes dwelling, other structures, personal property, loss of use, liability, and medical payments
  • Average Cost: $2,165 per year (significantly higher than the national average of $1,311)
  • Not Legally Required: But mortgage lenders almost always mandate it
  • Hurricane Deductibles: Separate from standard deductibles, typically 2-10% of insured value
  • Key Exclusions: Flood damage and sinkhole damage usually require separate policies

Florida homeowners face unique challenges when securing adequate insurance protection. With hurricane season lasting six months each year, torrential downpours occurring year-round, and more sinkholes than any other state in the nation, standard policies often need significant customization.

The insurance landscape in Florida has become increasingly complex, with premiums doubling in many areas over the past three years. Understanding what’s covered, what’s excluded, and how to steer the market is essential for protecting your home and financial security.

Many homeowners don’t find gaps in their coverage until it’s too late – after a hurricane has damaged their roof, floodwaters have entered their home, or a sinkhole has compromised their foundation. These scenarios can lead to devastating financial losses if your policy isn’t properly structured.

I’m Paul Schneider, owner of two independent insurance agencies in Florida representing over 50 insurance companies, with extensive experience helping homeowners steer Florida home insurance coverage options to find protection that balances affordability with comprehensive protection.

Is Homeowners Insurance Required in Florida?

The short answer: No, Florida state law doesn’t require you to have homeowners insurance. But before you celebrate this apparent freedom, there’s more to the story.

If you’re like most homeowners and have a mortgage, your lender will absolutely require you to maintain adequate Florida home insurance coverage. This isn’t just a suggestion—it’s typically a non-negotiable condition of your loan. Your mortgage company wants to protect their investment in your property, and they’ll need proof of insurance at closing and throughout your loan term.

“I’ve seen too many Florida homeowners find the hard way what happens without proper coverage,” says Paul Schneider of Schneider and Associates Insurance Agencies. “One serious storm can wipe out decades of home equity overnight. The question isn’t whether you can legally skip insurance—it’s whether you can afford the risk of going without it.”

For those fortunate enough to own their homes outright, insurance remains technically optional. But given Florida’s hurricane season, flood risks, and sinkhole potential, going without coverage is like swimming with sharks while wearing a meat suit—technically possible, but extraordinarily risky.

Florida home insurance coverage & the law

While Florida home insurance coverage isn’t mandated by state law, Florida has created important guardrails for the insurance marketplace. The Florida Office of Insurance Regulation oversees all insurers operating in the state, reviewing rate changes and monitoring financial stability to protect consumers.

One legal surprise that catches many Florida homeowners off guard is lender-placed insurance (sometimes called force-placed coverage). If your required coverage lapses—even accidentally—your mortgage company can purchase a policy on your behalf and simply add the premium to your mortgage bill. These force-placed policies typically cost much more while covering much less.

Force-placed policies protect primarily the lender’s interests, not yours, and can be surprisingly difficult to remove once in place. Florida law requires lenders to notify you before placing such coverage, but these notices often get overlooked until you spot the higher premium on your mortgage statement.

What to do if you can’t find coverage

Florida’s insurance landscape has grown increasingly challenging in recent years. Several carriers have left the state entirely, while others have become insolvent. If you’re struggling to find standard coverage, don’t panic—you have several options:

The Florida Market Assistance Plan (FMAP) was created by the legislature in 1985 specifically to help connect Floridians with available insurers when the standard market becomes difficult to steer. This state-run referral service can be an excellent first step.

Citizens Property Insurance Corporation serves as Florida’s “insurer of last resort” when no private market options exist. While Citizens was historically less expensive, recent rate increases have made it less affordable than in years past. Still, it provides a critical safety net for homeowners who might otherwise go uninsured.

Surplus lines insurance carriers operate outside standard state regulations but can offer coverage for high-risk properties when standard insurers won’t. While potentially more expensive, these specialized providers fill important coverage gaps in Florida’s challenging market.

Working with knowledgeable Florida home insurance agents who represent multiple carriers can make all the difference. As one relieved client recently told us: “After getting a non-renewal notice and being turned down by three companies, I was losing hope. My independent agent found an A-rated carrier I’d never heard of that offered better coverage at a price I could afford.”

The Department of Financial Services’ Homeowners Insurance Overview provides additional guidance for navigating Florida’s unique insurance landscape, including information about wind mitigation credits that can significantly reduce your premiums.

While Florida home insurance coverage isn’t legally required, going without proper protection puts your most valuable asset at tremendous risk in a state where Mother Nature regularly reminds us of her power.

What Does a Standard Florida Policy Cover?

Standard Florida home insurance coverage typically includes six primary components, often referred to as “coverages” and labeled A through F:

Coverage A: Dwelling

Protects the physical structure of your home, including attached structures like garages. This is typically the largest portion of your policy and serves as the basis for calculating other coverage limits.

Coverage B: Other Structures

Covers detached structures on your property, such as sheds, fences, and detached garages. This is usually set at 10% of your dwelling coverage.

Coverage C: Personal Property

Protects your belongings inside the home, including furniture, clothing, electronics, and other personal items. Typically set at 50-70% of dwelling coverage.

Coverage D: Loss of Use

Pays for additional living expenses if your home becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss. This might include hotel costs, restaurant meals, and other temporary expenses. Usually set at 20% of dwelling coverage.

Coverage E: Personal Liability

Protects you if someone is injured on your property or if you accidentally damage someone else’s property. Coverage typically starts at $100,000 but can be increased.

Coverage F: Medical Payments

Covers smaller medical bills for guests injured on your property, regardless of fault. Typically ranges from $1,000 to $5,000.

Covered perils versus exclusions

Understanding what events trigger coverage is crucial for Florida homeowners. Policies are structured as either:

Named Peril Policies: Cover only specifically listed events (HO-1, HO-2, HO-8)
Open Peril Policies: Cover all risks except those specifically excluded (HO-3, HO-5)

Most Florida homeowners have HO-3 policies, which provide open peril coverage for the dwelling but named peril coverage for personal property.

Typical covered perils include:
– Fire or lightning
– Windstorm or hail (including hurricane winds)
– Explosion
– Riot or civil commotion
– Aircraft damage
– Vehicle damage
– Smoke
– Vandalism and malicious mischief
– Theft
– Falling objects
– Weight of ice, snow, or sleet
– Accidental discharge of water or steam
– Sudden and accidental tearing apart of systems
– Freezing of household systems
– Sudden and accidental damage from electrical current

Critical exclusions in standard Florida home insurance coverage include:
– Flood damage (requires separate flood insurance)
– Earth movement (including sinkholes, except for catastrophic ground cover collapse)
– Normal wear and tear
– Intentional loss
– Neglect
– War
– Nuclear hazard
– Governmental action
– Ordinance or law (unless endorsed)
– Power failure (off-premises)
– Mold (limited coverage may be available)

“The most costly misunderstanding we see is homeowners assuming their policy covers flood damage,” notes Paul Schneider. “Florida receives an average of 60 inches of rainfall annually, making it more flood-prone than any other state, yet standard policies explicitly exclude flood damage.”

Filing claims under Florida timelines

Florida law provides specific protections and timelines for homeowners filing insurance claims:

  • Insurers must acknowledge claim communications within 7 calendar days
  • Companies must pay or deny property claims within 60 days of notice (unless circumstances beyond their control cause delays)
  • For hurricane claims, initial notices must be filed within 1 year, with supplemental claims within 18 months
  • The Homeowner Claims Bill of Rights provides additional consumer protections

If you experience claim delays or denials, the Florida Department of Financial Services offers a complaint process and mediation services.

Florida Home Insurance Coverage Options & Policy Types

Florida home insurance coverage comes in several standardized forms, each designed for different property types and needs:

HO-3 (Special Form)

The most common policy for single-family homes in Florida. If you’re like most Florida homeowners, this is probably what you have. It offers robust protection, covering your home on an open-peril basis (meaning everything is covered except what’s specifically excluded), while your personal belongings are covered for named perils only.

HO-5 (Comprehensive Form)

Think of this as the premium package for homeowners who want maximum protection. Not only does it cover your dwelling structure for all perils not specifically excluded, but it extends that same broad coverage to your personal property too. Many Florida homeowners appreciate that it typically includes replacement cost coverage for belongings without factoring in depreciation.

HO-6 (Condo Unit Owners Form)

Living in one of Florida’s many condominiums requires special coverage. These policies protect what your condo association’s master policy doesn’t – your personal belongings, liability, and the interior structure of your unit. Florida law has your back here, requiring all HO-6 policies to include at least $2,000 of loss assessment coverage with a deductible no higher than $250.

HO-4 (Renters Form)

For those renting in the Sunshine State, this policy protects what matters to you – your stuff and your liability. Since your landlord handles insuring the building itself, you can focus on protecting your personal property against theft, fire, and other covered perils.

HO-8 (Modified Coverage Form)

Florida’s historic homes in places like St. Augustine or Ybor City often need this specialized coverage. It’s designed for older homes (typically 40+ years) where rebuilding costs would exceed market value. Rather than replacement cost, these policies use actual cash value settlement methods.

DF-3 (Dwelling Form)

Own a rental property in Florida or a vacation home you only visit occasionally? This form primarily covers the structure with limited contents coverage, making it ideal for landlords and second-home owners.

Mobile Home Forms

With Florida’s significant population of manufactured housing, specialized mobile home policies address the unique risks these structures face compared to traditional construction.

Choosing the right form for Florida home insurance coverage

Finding the perfect policy type depends on your specific situation:

Your property type naturally guides your choice – single-family homes typically use HO-3 or HO-5, while condos need HO-6 coverage and renters benefit from HO-4 policies.

The age of your home matters tremendously in Florida. Newer homes qualify for standard forms, but if your beautiful Florida bungalow is over 40 years old, you might need the HO-8 form or need to make substantial updates to qualify for standard coverage.

How you use your property affects eligibility too. Primary residences, seasonal beach houses, and rental properties all have different requirements and rating factors.

Construction type significantly impacts your rates in hurricane-prone Florida. Masonry (block) construction typically qualifies for better rates than frame construction because it generally withstands wind better.

Your roof’s age and material can make or break your insurance options. Many Florida insurers have implemented strict requirements in recent years, sometimes refusing to cover homes with roofs older than 10-15 years.

“A common mistake we see is homeowners trying to force-fit their unique property into a standard policy,” explains Paul Schneider. “Florida’s diverse housing stock—from historic homes in St. Augustine to modern high-rises in Miami—requires custom coverage approaches.”

Customizing with endorsements

Think of your standard policy as a good foundation that often needs improvements through endorsements (also called riders) to provide truly comprehensive Florida home insurance coverage:

Water Backup Coverage protects against damage from sewer backups or sump pump failures, which standard policies exclude. This relatively inexpensive add-on can save you thousands if your neighborhood has aging infrastructure.

Ordinance & Law Coverage is particularly important in Florida, where building codes frequently change after major hurricanes. If your home suffers partial damage, this coverage helps pay the extra costs to rebuild according to current, stricter building codes.

Equipment Breakdown coverage feels like a blessing when your air conditioning system fails in the middle of a Florida summer. Standard policies typically don’t cover mechanical failures, making this endorsement valuable in our hot climate.

Scheduled Personal Property provides improved coverage for your treasured items like jewelry, art, or collectibles that exceed standard policy limits. This is particularly valuable for items with significant sentimental or monetary value.

Sinkhole Coverage addresses a risk that’s uniquely common in Florida. While catastrophic ground cover collapse is included in standard policies, comprehensive sinkhole protection requires an endorsement – especially worth considering in high-risk counties like Hernando, Pasco, and Hillsborough.

Identity Theft Protection covers expenses related to restoring your identity if stolen, an increasingly common problem that standard policies don’t address.

Umbrella Liability provides additional liability protection beyond standard policy limits, offering peace of mind if you have significant assets to protect.

A recent client in Newberry shared: “After getting a standard policy, our agent recommended adding water backup coverage since our neighborhood had aging infrastructure. Six months later, a main line backup flooded our finished basement. That $30 endorsement saved us over $15,000 in damages.”

The right endorsements transform a basic policy into comprehensive protection custom specifically to your Florida home’s unique risks and your personal needs. Costs, Discounts & Deductibles in the Sunshine State

The financial aspects of Florida home insurance coverage can be complex, with costs significantly higher than national averages:

Coverage TypeFlorida AverageNational AverageDifference
Homeowners$2,165$1,311+65%
Flood (NFIP)$550$700-21%
Umbrella ($1M)$300$3000%

Several factors drive Florida’s higher premiums:

  • Hurricane Risk: Florida’s 1,350 miles of coastline create substantial exposure to tropical storms.
  • Litigation Rates: Florida had 9% of homeowners insurance claims nationally but 79% of insurance lawsuits.
  • Reinsurance Costs: Insurers operating in Florida pay high rates to transfer risk to global reinsurance markets.
  • Fraud: Assignment of benefits abuse and fraudulent claims have driven up costs.
  • Construction Costs: Building materials and labor are more expensive in Florida.

Location within Florida also significantly impacts rates, with coastal properties often paying double what similar inland homes might pay.

Florida’s hurricane deductible explained

One unique feature of Florida home insurance coverage is the hurricane deductible. Unlike the standard dollar-amount deductible that applies to most claims, hurricane deductibles are percentage-based, typically ranging from 2% to 10% of your dwelling coverage.

For example, if your home is insured for $300,000 with a 2% hurricane deductible, you’d be responsible for the first $6,000 in hurricane damage before insurance pays.

Important aspects of Florida hurricane deductibles:

  • They apply only when the National Hurricane Center declares a hurricane
  • The deductible applies once per calendar year, not per storm
  • After paying one hurricane deductible, any subsequent hurricane claims that year would use your standard deductible
  • Florida law requires insurers to offer options ranging from 2% to 10%, with higher percentages resulting in lower premiums
  • Some lenders may limit how high your hurricane deductible can be

“Hurricane deductibles are one of the most misunderstood aspects of Florida policies,” says Paul Schneider. “Many homeowners are shocked to learn they’re responsible for thousands of dollars out-of-pocket after a hurricane. We recommend keeping emergency savings equal to your hurricane deductible.”

Saving money on Florida home insurance coverage

Despite high base rates, several strategies can help reduce Florida home insurance coverage costs:

Wind Mitigation Inspection: Florida law requires insurers to offer premium discounts for hurricane-resistant features. A certified inspector can document features like:
– Hurricane straps or clips connecting roof to walls
– Impact-resistant windows or hurricane shutters
– Reinforced garage doors
– Secondary water resistance barriers
– Construction built to post-2002 building codes

These inspections typically cost $100-200 but can save hundreds or thousands annually on premiums.

Multi-Policy Discounts: Bundling home and auto insurance with the same carrier often yields 5-15% savings.

Protective Devices: Discounts for security systems, smoke detectors, and water leak detection systems can reduce premiums.

Higher Deductibles: Opting for higher standard deductibles (not hurricane deductibles) can lower premiums, though you’ll pay more out-of-pocket for claims.

Maintain Good Credit: In Florida, insurers can use credit-based insurance scores to determine rates.

Shop Around: As an independent agency, we at Schneider and Associates Insurance Agencies can compare rates from multiple carriers to find the best value.

One Micco client shared: “After completing the wind mitigation inspection our agent recommended, our annual premium dropped by $780. The inspection cost $150, so we recouped the cost in less than three months.”

Managing Risks Not Covered by Standard Policies

When it comes to protecting your Florida home, standard Florida home insurance coverage leaves some significant gaps that need your attention. Think of these gaps as the fine print that can lead to major financial headaches after a disaster strikes.

Flood coverage options for Floridians

Let’s face it – Florida and flooding go hand in hand. With a whopping 60 inches of annual rainfall and our vulnerability to storm surge, Florida homes face more flood risk than properties in any other state. Yet standard homeowners policies explicitly exclude flood damage.

“I’ve seen too many families devastated when they find their regular policy won’t cover a penny of flood damage,” shares Paul Schneider. “Even just a few inches of floodwater can cause tens of thousands in damage to floors, walls, and belongings.”

Your main options for flood protection include:

The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) offers federally-backed coverage to any homeowner in participating communities (which includes most of Florida). This program provides up to $250,000 for your dwelling and $100,000 for contents, but comes with a 30-day waiting period before coverage kicks in. While the NFIP provides replacement cost coverage for your primary dwelling, it only offers actual cash value for contents and provides no coverage for additional living expenses if you’re displaced.

Private flood insurance has become increasingly available in Florida, often offering higher coverage limits, shorter waiting periods, additional living expense coverage, and potentially lower premiums for properties in lower-risk areas.

For high-value homes, excess flood coverage can provide protection above NFIP limits.

To check your property’s flood risk, visit the FEMA flood maps or use modern tools like the First Street Foundation’s Flood Factor. But here’s a sobering fact – about 25% of all flood claims come from properties outside designated high-risk zones.

Sinkhole versus catastrophic ground cover collapse

Florida’s limestone foundation makes us the sinkhole capital of America. While standard policies include coverage for catastrophic ground cover collapse (CGCC), this protection comes with a strict four-part definition:

  1. The abrupt collapse of ground cover
  2. A depression in the ground cover clearly visible to the naked eye
  3. Structural damage to the building including the foundation
  4. The insured structure being condemned and ordered to be vacated by the government

The problem? Many sinkholes don’t check all these boxes, leaving homeowners without protection unless they’ve purchased additional coverage.

Sinkhole endorsements provide broader protection but typically require geological testing, higher premiums, and larger deductibles. This coverage is especially worth considering if you live in high-risk counties like Hernando, Pasco, or Hillsborough.

If you end up in a dispute over a sinkhole claim, Florida offers a Neutral Evaluation program where independent engineers or geologists assess the damage and recommend repair methods – a helpful resource when you’re facing complex geological issues.

Other key endorsements

Several other coverage additions can be particularly valuable for Florida homeowners:

Water backup coverage protects against damage from sewer backups or sump pump failures – problems that standard policies typically exclude. One Orlando homeowner told us: “When our sewer line backed up during heavy rains, this $40 endorsement saved us over $8,000 in cleanup and repairs.”

Screen enclosure coverage is often overlooked until it’s too late. These pool cages and lanais are common features of Florida homes but often have severe limitations in standard policies. After Hurricane Ian, many homeowners were shocked to find their policies had a $2,500 limit for screen enclosures when replacement costs ran $15,000 or more.

Ordinance & law coverage becomes crucial after a major loss when rebuilding must meet current building codes, which are frequently updated in Florida following hurricanes. Without this endorsement, you might face thousands in out-of-pocket expenses to meet new elevation requirements, wind-resistance standards, or electrical codes.

Identity theft protection covers expenses related to restoring your identity if stolen – an increasingly common problem that standard policies don’t address.

The bottom line? Standard Florida home insurance coverage is just the starting point. Working with a knowledgeable local agent like those at Schneider and Associates Insurance Agencies can help identify your specific risk exposures and build a comprehensive protection plan that addresses Florida’s unique challenges.

Buying, Maintaining & Claiming: A Florida Roadmap

Navigating Florida home insurance coverage can feel like charting a course through hurricane season—complex and sometimes overwhelming. Let me walk you through the entire journey, from purchasing a policy to filing a claim, with some practical advice along the way.

Step-by-step: securing Florida home insurance coverage

The process of getting proper protection for your Florida home doesn’t have to be stressful. Start by gathering all the important details about your property—construction type, age, special features, and any previous claims. If your home is older (typically 30+ years), be prepared for additional scrutiny during the application process.

Working with an independent agent is truly your best bet. At Schneider and Associates, we can shop your home with multiple carriers simultaneously, each with different appetites for Florida’s unique risks. What one insurer might decline, another might welcome with competitive rates.

“I always recommend getting inspections done before applying,” says Paul Schneider. “A 4-point inspection covering your roof, electrical, plumbing, and HVAC systems, along with a wind mitigation inspection, can prevent unpleasant surprises during underwriting and might identify discount opportunities right from the start.”

When reviewing coverage options, take time to understand what limits make sense for your situation. Your dwelling coverage should reflect rebuilding costs (not market value or your mortgage amount), and your liability protection should align with your overall financial picture.

Be carefully honest on your application—accidental misrepresentations about your home’s features or condition can lead to denied claims down the road. Once your policy is bound, prepare for the insurer’s inspection, which typically follows within 30 days. Address any required repairs promptly to maintain coverage.

If you have a mortgage, coordinate with your lender to set up proper escrow for insurance payments. And always keep digital and physical copies of your policy documents where you can access them quickly after a disaster.

What to expect after a loss

When damage occurs to your home, knowing how to steer the claims process can make all the difference in your recovery experience.

First, report your claim promptly. Florida’s intense heat and humidity can quickly worsen initial damage, particularly with water-related losses. Most insurers offer 24/7 claims reporting through apps, websites, or phone lines.

Before you touch anything, document everything thoroughly. Take photos and videos from multiple angles showing the full extent of the damage. This visual evidence becomes invaluable if disputes arise later about pre-existing conditions.

Don’t wait for the insurance adjuster before making emergency repairs to prevent further damage. If your roof is leaking, covering it with a tarp is reasonable and expected. Keep all receipts for materials and services, as these emergency measures are typically reimbursable under your policy.

When the adjuster schedules their visit, make sure you’re present during the inspection. Point out all damage areas and provide your documentation. Take notes during this meeting and get the adjuster’s contact information for follow-up questions.

Under Florida law, insurers must acknowledge claims within 7 days and pay or deny claims within 60 days unless circumstances beyond their control cause delays. The Homeowner Claims Bill of Rights provides additional protections for Florida residents.

If you find yourself at odds with your insurer over the claim amount, consider using Florida’s free mediation program through the Department of Financial Services before hiring an attorney. For particularly complex claims, licensed public adjusters can help negotiate on your behalf, though they typically charge a percentage of the settlement.

A client from Palm Coast recently shared: “After Hurricane Ian damaged our lanai and roof, we felt overwhelmed by the paperwork and insurance terminology. Our agent translated everything into plain English and helped us document our claim properly. We received a fair settlement within 45 days, which was such a relief during a stressful time.”

The best approach to claims is preparedness before disaster strikes. Creating a home inventory with photos or video of your belongings, saving receipts for major purchases, and understanding your policy’s provisions will position you for a smoother claims experience if the unthinkable happens.

For more comprehensive guidance, the Florida Department of Financial Services offers an excellent Homeowners Insurance Toolkit with worksheets and checklists to help you through the process. Frequently Asked Questions about Florida Home Insurance Coverage

Is homeowners insurance legally mandatory in Florida?

No, Florida state law doesn’t require you to have homeowners insurance. It’s one of those interesting quirks of our state regulations – you technically can own a home without insurance coverage.

But here’s the reality: if you have a mortgage (like most homeowners do), your lender will absolutely require you to maintain adequate insurance. They’ve invested in your property alongside you, and they want that investment protected.

“I often explain to clients that while the state won’t force you to buy coverage, going without insurance in Florida is like driving a car without brakes,” says Paul Schneider. “You might be fine for a while, but when something goes wrong, the consequences are devastating.”

Even for those fortunate enough to own their homes outright, the financial risk of going uninsured in our hurricane-prone state is simply too great for most to bear. One severe storm could wipe out your life’s investment overnight.

How does the hurricane deductible differ from a standard deductible?

This is one of the most important distinctions in Florida home insurance coverage – and unfortunately, many homeowners don’t fully understand it until they need to file a claim.

Your standard deductible is a fixed dollar amount – typically $1,000 or $2,500 – that applies to most claims like fire, theft, or water damage. But hurricane deductibles work completely differently:

They’re calculated as a percentage of your dwelling coverage (usually 2-10%), not a flat dollar amount. For a home insured for $300,000 with a 2% hurricane deductible, you’d be responsible for the first $6,000 in hurricane damage.

Hurricane deductibles only activate when damage is caused by a named hurricane as declared by the National Hurricane Center. The good news is that this deductible applies just once per calendar year. If you’re unfortunate enough to be hit by multiple hurricanes in the same year, only the first would trigger the percentage deductible – subsequent hurricane claims would use your standard deductible.

Many Tampa homeowners learned this lesson the hard way during recent hurricane seasons. One client told us: “I had no idea my hurricane deductible was $8,400 until after the storm hit. I wish I’d understood this before setting up my emergency fund.”

Does a standard policy cover flood or sinkhole damage?

This is perhaps the most dangerous misconception about Florida home insurance coverage – and it’s a mistake that costs homeowners millions every year.

Standard homeowners policies explicitly exclude flood damage, regardless of the cause. It doesn’t matter if the water came from a hurricane storm surge, heavy rainfall, or overflowing lakes and rivers – if it’s rising water entering your home from the ground up, it’s considered flooding and it’s not covered.

You need separate flood insurance, available through either the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or private insurers. Don’t make the mistake of assuming you’re safe if you’re not in a “high-risk” zone – about 25% of all flood claims come from properties in low or moderate-risk areas.

As for sinkholes, standard policies offer very limited protection. They cover only “catastrophic ground cover collapse,” which has a strict four-part definition that many sinkholes don’t meet. For comprehensive sinkhole protection, especially if you’re in high-risk counties like Hernando, Pasco, or Hillsborough, you’ll need a specific sinkhole endorsement.

“The saddest conversations I have are with homeowners who thought they were fully protected,” shares Paul Schneider. “After 20 years in this business, I can tell you that understanding these exclusions before disaster strikes can mean the difference between recovery and financial ruin.”
