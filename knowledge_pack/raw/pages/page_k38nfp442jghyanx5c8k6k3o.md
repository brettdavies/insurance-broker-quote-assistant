### What State Does Not Require Car Insurance?
In New Hampshire, buying car insurance is optional, but drivers must prove they can cover damages if they cause an accident. Drivers who choose to get coverage must meet New Hampshire's state minimum requirements:
  * $25,000 for bodily injury per person
  * $50,000 per accident
  * $25,000 for property damage
  * Uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage matching liability limits
  * At least $1,000 in medical payments coverage


### State Minimum Coverage Requirements Explained
Every state has its own laws mandating state minimum car insurance, and each state determines which [types of coverage](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/coverage/) and policy limits are required. Understanding what these different coverage options actually do helps you stay on the right side of the law and figure out how much financial protection makes sense for your situation.
![blueCheckmark icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/blue_checkmark_cucw0x.svg)
Liability insurance
Liability insurance forms the foundation of state minimum car insurance in all states except New Hampshire.
**What it covers:** Pays for damage you cause to other people and their property
    * **Bodily Injury Liability:** Medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering when you injure someone
    * **Property Damage Liability:** Repair/replacement costs for other people's vehicles and property
_*Required in all states except New Hampshire_
![blueCheckmark icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/blue_checkmark_cucw0x.svg)
Uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage
**What it covers:** Protects you when hit by a driver with no insurance or insufficient coverage
    * [**Uninsured Motorist**](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/what-is-uninsured-motorist-insurance-and-do-you-need-it/)**:** For drivers with no insurance
    * **Underinsured Motorist:** For drivers whose coverage is insufficient
**Coverage amounts:** Usually match your state's minimum liability requirements
_*Required in 22 states_
![blueCheckmark icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/blue_checkmark_cucw0x.svg)
Personal injury protection (PIP)
Personal injury protection coverage is required as part of state minimum car insurance in 12 no-fault states: Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota and Utah.
**What it covers:** Your own medical expenses and lost wages, regardless of who caused the accident
**Typical benefits:**
    * 80% of medical costs up to policy limit
    * Percentage of lost wages
    * Essential services (childcare, housekeeping)
    * Rehabilitation expenses
![blueCheckmark icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/blue_checkmark_cucw0x.svg)
Medical payments coverage (MedPay)
**What it covers:** Medical expenses from car accidents regardless of fault
**Key differences from PIP:**
    * Lower coverage limits ($1,000 to $10,000)
    * Covers only medical and funeral expenses
    * No lost wages or other benefits
    * Supplements [health insurance](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/health/)
_*Required in three states: Maine, New Hampshire and Pennsylvania_


#### No-Fault vs. At-Fault States
Where you live determines how car insurance works after an accident. Some states require each driver to use their own insurance first (no-fault), while others assign financial responsibility to whoever caused the accident (at-fault).
![uninsured icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/uninsured_jck1df.svg)
No-Fault States (12 states)
Each driver's insurance pays for their own medical expenses, regardless of who caused the accident
**Required coverage:** PIP mandatory states include: Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota and Utah
![insurance2 icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/insurance_2_iixd7m.svg)
At-Fault States (38 states)
The driver who caused the accident pays for everyone's injuries and damages
**Required coverage:** Generally only liability insurance 
**Additional requirements:** Many of these states require uninsured motorist protection


### Alternatives to State Minimum Car Insurance
While most states require traditional [auto insurance](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/), some allow alternative ways to meet financial responsibility laws:
![balanceTransfer icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/balanceTransfer_evfl14.svg)
**Cash deposit programs**
Some states let drivers deposit $30,000 to $75,000 with the DMV instead of buying car insurance. This deposit acts as a guarantee for accident claims and is held in an interest-bearing account. Drivers must show proof of substantial liquid assets beyond the deposit to qualify. These funds remain tied up and unavailable for other use.
![bond icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/bond_sjrp2s.svg)
**Surety bond programs**
A surety bond, between $30,000 and $75,000, can satisfy legal requirements in certain states. Bonding companies guarantee payment for claims, but drivers must pay premiums and reimburse the company for any payouts. Most companies require a credit score above 650 and proof of steady income. Unlike deposits, bonds don't lock up your capital.
![insuranceCheck icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/insurance_t2aozs.svg)
**Self-insurance certificates**
For large businesses or fleet operators, self-insurance requires a net worth over $1 million and reserves of $500,000 to $1 million. States require fleets of 10 to 26+ vehicles. Drivers must prove they can cover potential liability without car insurance.


Check with your state's DMV or insurance department to see which alternatives are available and what's required to qualify.
![mglogo icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/mg-logo_wxc8wu.svg)
GEOGRAPHIC LIMITATIONS OF ALTERNATIVE INSURANCE OPTIONS
Alternative insurance options are typically valid only in the issuing state, creating complications for drivers who travel often or relocate. If you move to a state that doesn't recognize your alternative coverage, you must obtain traditional car insurance or face penalties for driving without coverage.

### Key Factors Affecting Minimum Car Insurance Rates
Understanding factors affecting car insurance cost helps explain why you might pay more or less than the state average and identifies where you can reduce your premiums.
![teen icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/teen_xv2qqk.svg)
**Age and experience impact**
Young drivers pay more for state minimum car insurance policies because of higher accident rates and lack of driving experience. Teenage drivers pay two to three times more than adult drivers for the same coverage.
Drivers under 25 face the highest premiums, with costs gradually decreasing as drivers gain experience. Senior drivers over 65 may see slight increases because of slower reaction times but generally pay less than younger drivers.
![usMap icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/us-map_s1ingz.svg)
**Location makes a major difference**
Your ZIP code impacts your state's minimum car insurance costs because of varying accident rates, theft statistics and repair costs. Urban areas cost more than rural locations because of higher traffic density and increased theft rates. States with no-fault insurance laws or high litigation rates also raise minimum coverage costs.
![badCredit icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/badCredit_e542cx.svg)
**Driving record and credit score**
Your driving history is one of the strongest predictors of car insurance cost. A single at-fault accident can increase premiums by 20% to 50%, while DUI convictions can double or triple costs. Multiple violations create compounding effects.
Credit scores also impact rates in states where allowed, with poor credit increasing premiums. California, Hawaii, Massachusetts and Michigan prohibit using credit scores as a rating factor.
![car2 icon](https://res.cloudinary.com/moneygeek/image/upload/v1585171420/assets/components/icon/car2_xvl57e.svg)
**Vehicle type and usage**
The car you drive affects how much you'll pay for minimum car insurance coverage, even though [liability coverage](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/what-is-liability-insurance-coverage/) doesn't protect your vehicle. Sports cars, luxury vehicles and cars with high theft rates cost more to insure because they're statistically more likely to be involved in serious accidents.
Annual mileage also matters. Drivers who commute long distances face higher premiums because of increased accident exposure.

## Is State Minimum Car Insurance Enough? 
Minimum coverage meets states' legal requirements but may not fully protect your finances. Assess whether a minimum coverage policy provides adequate protection:
**Liability Coverage:** Your liability limits should at least match your net worth, including home equity, savings and investments. If you cause $500,000 in damages but only have $50,000 in coverage, you're on the hook for the rest.
**Medical Coverage:** In at-fault states, you may have no immediate medical coverage while fault is determined. In no-fault states, ensure your PIP limits cover extended care and lost wages. Factor in your health insurance deductible and out-of-pocket maximums.
**Vehicle Protection:** If your car were totaled, could you afford to replace it? If not, comprehensive and collision coverage may be worth the cost, especially if you're still making payments or rely heavily on your vehicle.

### Minimum Car Insurance Requirements: FAQ
We answer common questions about state minimum car insurance and coverage needs:
### What happens if you only have minimum coverage in an accident?
If you only have minimum car insurance coverage and cause a serious accident, you could be personally responsible for damages exceeding your policy limits. For example, if you cause $100,000 in damages but only have $25,000 in property damage coverage, you'd owe $75,000 out of pocket.
The other person could sue you for the remaining amount, potentially resulting in wage garnishment or asset seizure. Minimum coverage also doesn't include comprehensive or collision coverage, meaning your own vehicle repairs wouldn't be covered.
### What states are no-fault insurance states?
The 12 [no-fault insurance](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/state-minimum-car-insurance-requirements/#no-fault-vs-at-fault) states are Delaware, Florida, Hawaii, Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota and Utah. These states require PIP coverage, which pays for your own medical expenses regardless of who caused the accident. Three states (Kentucky, New Jersey and Pennsylvania) are considered choice no-fault states, allowing drivers to choose between no-fault benefits or the right to sue for damages.
### What happens if I move to a different state?
You must update your car insurance to meet your new state's requirements, usually within 30 days. Your new state may require different coverage types or higher limits. For example, moving from a state that doesn't require PIP coverage to a no-fault state means you'll need to add PIP to your policy. Contact your insurance company immediately after moving to ensure your policy complies with your new state's laws and avoid potential penalties or coverage gaps.
### What if I only have minimum coverage and get hit by an uninsured driver? 
Your protection depends on whether your state requires uninsured motorist coverage. If your state mandates this coverage, your car insurance will pay for your injuries and damages up to your policy limits. If uninsured motorist coverage isn't required in your state and you didn't purchase it, you may have to pay for your own medical expenses and vehicle repairs or pursue legal action against the uninsured driver to recover costs.
### Is minimum car insurance enough?
Minimum car insurance provides basic legal compliance but is often [insufficient](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/state-minimum-car-insurance-requirements/#is-minimum-coverage-enough) for comprehensive financial protection. State minimums are designed to ensure all drivers have some coverage, not to provide adequate protection for serious accidents. If you have assets to protect or can't afford to replace your vehicle, you likely need coverage beyond state minimums.
### What's the difference between state minimum and full coverage? 
State minimum coverage includes only the legally required liability insurance and any mandatory coverage like PIP or uninsured motorist protection. [Full coverage](https://www.moneygeek.com/insurance/auto/state-minimum-car-insurance-requirements/#minimum-vs-full-coverage) includes state minimums plus comprehensive and collision coverage for your own vehicle, higher liability limits and additional optional coverage. Full coverage provides comprehensive financial protection for both liability and physical damage to your vehicle.
### What happens if my insurance lapses? 
If your car insurance lapses, you face immediate consequences, including license suspension, vehicle registration suspension, fines and SR-22 filing requirements. Many states have electronic monitoring systems that detect when coverage ends.
Even a brief lapse can result in penalties. If your car insurance lapses and you're in an accident, you'll be personally responsible for all damages and could face additional criminal charges for driving without insurance.
