Types of Masonry Construction For Commercial Buildings Explained

LH TeamApril 18, 2024January 25th, 20258 min read

Masonry stands as a cornerstone of commercial construction, offering durability, aesthetic appeal, and structural integrity.

This guide navigates through various types of masonry commonly employed in commercial buildings, including brick, concrete, stone, stucco, composite, and precast masonry. Each type brings its own set of advantages and limitations, catering to diverse architectural needs and design preferences.

Different Types of Masonry For Commercial Construction

Brick Masonry

Bricks, renowned for their timeless charm and robustness, have been a staple in commercial construction for centuries. They come in various types, sizes, and colors, offering architects and builders a versatile palette to work with.

Common brick bonds such as running bonds, Flemish bonds, and English bonds add to the visual appeal while ensuring structural stability. Despite its durability and fire resistance, brick masonry may require skilled labor and meticulous maintenance.

Concrete Masonry

Concrete blocks, known for their strength and load-bearing capabilities, are prevalent in modern commercial buildings. These blocks can be solid, hollow, or cellular, offering flexibility in design and construction.

Reinforced concrete masonry walls enhance structural performance, making them suitable for high-rise structures. While concrete masonry provides excellent thermal insulation and fire resistance, it may require adequate waterproofing measures and periodic inspections.

Stone Masonry

Natural stone, prized for its unique textures and durability, adds a touch of elegance to commercial buildings. Various types of stone, such as granite, limestone, and marble, offer distinct visual characteristics and structural properties. Skilled craftsmen employ techniques like rubble masonry and ashlar masonry to create stunning facades and intricate details. Despite its aesthetic appeal and longevity, stone masonry may be cost-prohibitive and require specialized maintenance.

Stucco Masonry

Stucco, a versatile material made from cement, sand, and lime, is popular for its smooth finish and weather-resistant properties. Applied in layers, stucco can be textured or colored to achieve different aesthetics. Its lightweight nature makes it suitable for both interior and exterior applications in commercial construction. However, stucco masonry may be prone to cracking and requires regular inspections to maintain its integrity.

Composite Masonry

Composite masonry combines multiple materials such as brick, stone, and concrete to leverage their strengths. This approach allows for creative designs, enhanced structural performance, and cost-effective construction.

Composite masonry panels offer a balance between aesthetics and functionality, making them ideal for modern commercial architecture. However, proper detailing and compatibility between materials are crucial to ensure long-term durability.

Precast Masonry

Precast masonry involves manufacturing building components off-site and assembling them on-site, offering speed and precision in construction. Precast concrete panels, in particular, provide structural stability, weather resistance, and design flexibility.

Their modular nature allows for efficient installation, reducing construction time and labor costs. However, transportation logistics and coordination during assembly are key considerations for successful precast masonry projects.

Masonry Veneer vs. Solid Masonry

Masonry veneer, consisting of a thin layer of masonry attached to a structural frame, offers aesthetic appeal without bearing significant loads. Solid masonry, on the other hand, comprises thick walls made entirely of masonry units, providing both structural support and thermal mass.

The choice between veneer and solid masonry depends on factors such as building design, budget, and engineering requirements. Proper installation and detailing ensure the performance and longevity of both systems.

In commercial construction, the choice between masonry veneer and solid masonry plays a crucial role in determining both the aesthetic appeal and structural integrity of a building. Each option offers distinct advantages and considerations, making it essential to understand their key differences before making a decision.

Masonry Veneer

Masonry veneer refers to a thin layer of masonry material, such as brick or stone, that is attached to a structural frame or backup wall. This method allows for a wide range of design possibilities, as the veneer can be customized in terms of color, texture, and pattern. It is often used to enhance the exterior appearance of a building without bearing significant structural loads.

Advantages of Masonry Veneer

Aesthetic Versatility: Veneers offer flexibility in design, allowing architects to achieve diverse architectural styles and visual effects.

Cost-Effectiveness: Veneers are generally lighter and require less material compared to solid masonry, resulting in cost savings.

Thermal Performance: Veneers can contribute to the thermal efficiency of a building when combined with insulation in the cavity between the veneer and the backup wall.

Construction Efficiency: Installation of masonry veneer is typically faster and requires less labor compared to solid masonry.

Considerations for Masonry Veneer

Structural Support: Veneers rely on the structural integrity of the backup wall or frame and do not provide significant structural support on their own.

Maintenance: Proper detailing and maintenance are essential to prevent water infiltration and ensure the longevity of masonry veneer systems.

Compatibility: The compatibility of materials between the veneer and the backup wall is crucial to avoid potential issues such as differential movement.

Solid Masonry

Solid masonry, also known as load-bearing masonry, consists of thick walls made entirely of masonry units such as bricks or concrete blocks. These walls provide both structural support and thermal mass, contributing to the overall stability and energy efficiency of a building.

Advantages of Solid Masonry

Structural Strength: Solid masonry walls are capable of bearing significant vertical and lateral loads, making them suitable for multi-story buildings.

Durability: Solid masonry offers excellent resistance to fire, moisture, and pests, contributing to the long-term durability of a structure.

Sound Insulation: The mass of solid masonry walls provides effective sound insulation, reducing noise transmission between interior spaces.

Minimal Maintenance: Compared to veneers, solid masonry requires less ongoing maintenance and is less susceptible to moisture-related issues.

Considerations for Solid Masonry

Design Limitations: Solid masonry may limit design flexibility compared to veneers, particularly in terms of exterior finishes and detailing.

Construction Time: Building solid masonry walls can be more time-consuming and labor-intensive than installing veneers, impacting project timelines.

Thermal Performance: Without additional insulation, solid masonry walls may have lower thermal efficiency compared to veneers combined with cavity insulation.

Ultimately, the choice between masonry veneer and solid masonry depends on factors such as architectural design, structural requirements, budget constraints, and desired performance characteristics. Collaboration between architects, engineers, and contractors is crucial to selecting the most appropriate masonry system for each commercial construction project.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the 4 types of masonry walls?

The four types of masonry walls are:

  1. Solid Masonry Walls: These walls are constructed using solid units like bricks or stones without any hollow spaces.
  2. Cavity Masonry Walls: These walls have an inner and outer layer of masonry units separated by a cavity. The cavity helps in preventing moisture penetration.
  3. Composite Masonry Walls: These walls combine different types of masonry units, such as bricks and concrete blocks, to enhance strength and insulation properties.
  4. Hollow Masonry Walls: These walls have hollow spaces within the masonry units, which can be filled with insulation materials to improve thermal performance.

What are the three types of masonry?

The three main types of masonry are:

  1. Brick Masonry: Constructed using bricks bonded together with mortar.
  2. Stone Masonry: Built using natural stones such as granite, marble, or limestone, often with mortar.
  3. Concrete Masonry: Made from concrete blocks or precast concrete units joined together with mortar.

Which concrete is used for commercial building?

Commercial buildings typically use reinforced concrete, which is concrete embedded with steel bars or mesh to enhance its strength and load-bearing capacity. This type of concrete is commonly used in construction due to its durability and versatility.

What are the 5 materials of masonry construction?

The five main materials used in masonry construction are:

  1. Bricks: Clay or concrete blocks used as primary building units.
  2. Stones: Natural stones such as granite, marble, limestone, or sandstone.
  3. Concrete Blocks: Pre-cast or molded concrete units used in masonry construction.
  4. Mortar: A mixture of cement, sand, and water used to bind masonry units together.
  5. Reinforcement: Steel bars or mesh used to strengthen masonry structures, especially in reinforced concrete construction.

Which Concrete is Used for Commercial Building?

The most commonly used type of concrete for commercial buildings is reinforced concrete. This type of concrete is strengthened with steel reinforcement bars (rebar) or fibers to improve its tensile strength and durability. Reinforced concrete is preferred because it offers a high degree of strength, durability, and flexibility in design. It can withstand significant loads, resist environmental stresses, and be molded into various shapes and forms, making it suitable for a wide range of commercial construction applications.

What is Class B Masonry?

Class B masonry refers to a classification of masonry work that typically denotes a standard level of quality and workmanship suitable for general construction purposes. It commonly includes materials such as standard concrete blocks, bricks, stone, and mortar. Class B masonry is typically used in the construction of walls, foundations, and other structural elements where a standard level of quality is acceptable, often found in residential, commercial, and light industrial buildings.
