A **home** , or **domicile** , is a [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space") used as a permanent or semi-permanent residence for one or more [human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human") occupants, and sometimes various [companion animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pet "Pet"). Homes provide sheltered spaces, for instance [rooms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rooms "Rooms"), where domestic activity can be performed such as sleeping, preparing food, eating and hygiene as well as providing spaces for work and leisure such as remote working, studying and playing. 
Physical forms of homes can be static such as a [house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House") or an [apartment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartment "Apartment"), mobile such as a [houseboat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houseboat "Houseboat"), [trailer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trailer_\(vehicle\) "Trailer \(vehicle\)") or [yurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yurt "Yurt") or digital such as virtual space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-1) The aspect of 'home' can be considered across scales; from the micro scale showcasing the most intimate spaces of the individual dwelling and direct surrounding area to the macro scale of the geographic area such as [town](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Town "Town"), [village](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Village "Village"), [city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City "City"), [country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Country "Country") or [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"). 
The concept of 'home' has been researched and theorized across disciplines – topics ranging from the idea of home, the interior, the psyche, liminal space, contested space to gender and politics.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-2) The home as a concept expands beyond residence as contemporary lifestyles and technological advances redefine the way the global population lives and works. The concept and experience encompasses the likes of [exile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exile "Exile"), yearning, [belonging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belongingness "Belongingness"), homesickness and homelessness.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-3)
## History
### Prehistoric
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/250px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg)[Taíno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno "Taíno") [petroglyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroglyph "Petroglyph") in a cave in Puerto Rico
The earliest homes humans inhabited were likely naturally occurring features such as [caves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave "Cave"). There is numerous evidence for early human species inhabiting caves from at least one million years ago, including _[Homo erectus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_erectus "Homo erectus")_ in China at [Zhoukoudian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhoukoudian "Zhoukoudian"), _[Homo rhodesiensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_rhodesiensis "Homo rhodesiensis")_ in South Africa at the Cave of Hearths ([Makapansgat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makapansgat "Makapansgat")), _[Homo neanderthalensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_neanderthalensis "Homo neanderthalensis")_ and _[Homo heidelbergensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis "Homo heidelbergensis")_ in Europe at [Archaeological Site of Atapuerca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeological_Site_of_Atapuerca "Archaeological Site of Atapuerca"), _[Homo floresiensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_floresiensis "Homo floresiensis")_ in Indonesia, and the [Denisovans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denisovans "Denisovans") in southern Siberia. 
In southern Africa, early modern humans regularly used sea caves as shelter starting about 180,000 years ago, when they learned to exploit the sea for the first time.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-4) The oldest known site is PP13B at [Pinnacle Point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinnacle_Point "Pinnacle Point"). This may have allowed rapid expansion of humans out of Africa and colonization of areas of the world such as Australia by 60–50,000 years ago. Throughout southern Africa, Australia, and Europe, early modern humans used caves and rock shelters as sites for rock art, such as those at [Giants Castle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giants_Castle "Giants Castle"). Caves such as the [yaodong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaodong "Yaodong") in China were used for shelter; other caves were used for burials (such as [rock-cut tombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock-cut_tombs "Rock-cut tombs")), or as religious sites (such as [Buddhist caves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Buddhist_caves "Category:Buddhist caves")). Among the known sacred caves are China's Cave of a Thousand Buddhas[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-5) and the [sacred caves of Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_caves_of_Crete "Sacred caves of Crete"). As technology progressed, humans and other hominids began constructing their own dwellings. Buildings such as [huts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hut_\(dwelling\) "Hut \(dwelling\)") and [longhouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longhouse "Longhouse") have been used for living since the late [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-Skara_Brae-6)
### Ancient
By the Bronze Age (c. 3500–1200 BC), communities in [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") began constructing permanent dwellings of [mudbrick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mudbrick "Mudbrick"); excavations at [Uruk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uruk "Uruk") and [Ubaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ubaid "Ubaid") reveal single-room and multi-room houses organised around small courtyards, built with uniform bricks and bitumen mortar.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-7) These early urban homes often clustered along straight streets and shared common wells and ovens.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-8) In [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt"), from the Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BC) onwards, town layouts at [Amarna](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Amarna,_Egypt&action=edit&redlink=1 "Amarna, Egypt \(page does not exist\)") and [Deir el-Medina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deir_el-Medina "Deir el-Medina") display mudbrick houses with flat roofs built in dense rows off narrow lanes; typical houses comprised a reception room, private chambers, and a small courtyard used for food preparation and work activities.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-9) The [Indus Valley Civilisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation "Indus Valley Civilisation") (c. 2600–1900 BC) featured standardised fired-bricks and sophisticated urban planning in cities like [Mohenjo-daro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohenjo-daro "Mohenjo-daro") and [Harappa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harappa "Harappa"), where two-story houses included private wells, indoor bathrooms with drainage, and south-facing courtyards engineered for ventilation in the hot climate.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-10)
On Bronze Age [Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete "Crete"), the Minoan palace at [Knossos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knossos "Knossos") incorporated residential quarters with light wells and lustral basins, reflecting an emphasis on light and ritual purity in domestic space.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-11) Surrounding settlements adopted similar rectilinear house plans centered on storage magazines and communal courts. By the 1st century BC in [Ancient Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"), the affluent lived in [domus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_villa "Roman villa")—multiroom urban houses built around an atrium and peristyle garden—while the majority resided in multi-story apartment blocks called insulae, often cramped and prone to fire hazards.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-12)
### Post-classical
After the fall of the Western Roman Empire in the 5th century, domestic architecture in Europe reverted to simple timber-framed or wattle-and-daub huts, while the elite continued to inhabit stone manor houses with great halls and defensive features. By the 12th century, these manor houses commonly featured a central hall, private solar chambers, and adjoining service wings, reflecting both social hierarchy and the need for local defense.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-13) In medieval towns, multi-storey timber-framed "hall houses" with jettied upper floors lined narrow streets, maximizing limited urban plots and providing shelter from street traffic.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-14)
Concurrently, in the Islamic world from the 8th century onwards, the inward-facing courtyard house became predominant. Private residences were organized around shaded central courts with water features, mashrabiya screens for ventilation and privacy, and richly decorated plasterwork and tile.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-15) In East Asia, the Chinese siheyuan compound—standardized during the Yuan and Ming dynasties—offered multigenerational living around a north–south axis courtyard, with ancillary rooms for servants and extended family. 
The Renaissance (14th–17th centuries) brought classical ideals into domestic design. In Florence, the Palazzo Medici Riccardi (begun 1444) introduced rusticated façades, symmetrical floor plans, and internal loggias, while Venetian villas by Palladio emphasized proportion, harmony, and integration with landscaped gardens.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-16) Advances in glassmaking allowed larger, clearer windows, and masonry chimneys gradually replaced central hearths, vastly improving light and air quality within homes.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-17) From the 14th to the 16th century, homelessness was perceived of as a "vagrancy problem" and legislative responses to the problem were predicated upon the threat it may pose to the state.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18)
### Modern era
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg/250px-Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Worker_home_from_1911_in_Helsinki.jpg)Industrialization brought mass migration to cities. This one-room worker home from [Helsinki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helsinki "Helsinki") is typical to late 19th century and early 20th century, often housing large families.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-19)
According to [Kirsten Gram-Hanssen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirsten_Gram-Hanssen "Kirsten Gram-Hanssen"), "It can be argued that historically and cross-culturally there is not always [a] strong relation between the concept of home and the physical building, and that this mode of thinking is rooted in [the Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") of the seventeenth century".[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:0-20) Before, one's home was more public than private; traits such as privacy, intimacy and familiarity would proceed to achieve greater prominence, aligning the concept with the [bourgeoisie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourgeoisie "Bourgeoisie").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-22) The connection between home and house was reinforced by a [case law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Case_law "Case law") declaration from [Edward Coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Coke "Edward Coke"): "The house of everyman is to him as his castle and fortress, as well as his defense against injury and violence, as for his repose". Colloquially, this was adapted into the phrase "The Englishman's home is his castle" which popularised the notion of home as house.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:2-23)
A result of the longstanding association between home and women, 18th century English women, of upper-class status, were scorned for pursuing activities outside of the home, thus seen to be of undesirable character.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:10-24) The concept of home took on unprecedent prominence by the 18th century, reified by cultural practice.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:11-25)
The concept of a [smart home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_automation "Home automation") arose in the 19th century in turn with electricity having been introduced to homes in a limited capacity.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:0-20) The distinction between home and work formulated in the 20th century, with home acting as sanctuary.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-26) Modern definitions portray home as a site of supreme comfort and familial intimacy, operating as a buffer to the greater world.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:10-24)
## Common types
Further information: [List of house types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_house_types "List of house types")
The concept of home is one with multiple interpretations, influenced by one's history and identity.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-27) People of differing ages, genders, ethnicities and classes may have resultingly different meanings of home.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:9-28) Commonly, it is associated with various forms of abodes such as wagons, cars, boats or tents although it is equally considered to extend beyond the space, in mind and emotion.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:6-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:8-30) The space of a home need not be significant or fixed though the boundaries of home are often tied to the space.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:6-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:8-30) There have been multiple theories regarding one's choice of home with the residential conditions of their childhood often reflected in their later choice of home.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21) According to [Paul Oliver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Oliver "Paul Oliver"), the vast majority of abodes are [vernacular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernacular_architecture "Vernacular architecture"), constructed in accordance with the residents' needs.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-CoolenEditorial-31)
### House
Main article: [House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House")
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg/250px-8A%2C_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_%2C_Bucharest_%28Romania%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:8A,_Bulevardul_Aviatorilor_,_Bucharest_\(Romania\).jpg)House at 8A, Bulevardul Aviatorilor, Bucharest, Romania
A house is a single-unit residential [building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building "Building"). It may range in complexity from a rudimentary [hut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hut "Hut") to a complex structure of [wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood "Wood"), [masonry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masonry "Masonry"), [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete "Concrete") or other material, outfitted with [plumbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plumbing "Plumbing"), electrical, and [heating, ventilation, and air conditioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heating,_ventilation,_and_air_conditioning "Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning") systems.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-schoenauer-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-33)
The social unit that lives in a house is known as a [household](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Household "Household"). Most commonly, a household is a [family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family "Family") unit of some kind, although households may also be other [social groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_\(sociology\) "Group \(sociology\)"), such as [roommates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roommate "Roommate") or, in a [rooming house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rooming_house "Rooming house"), unconnected individuals. Some houses only have a dwelling space for one family or similar-sized group; larger houses called [townhouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Townhouse "Townhouse") or [row houses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_house "Row house") may contain numerous family dwellings in the same structure. A house may be accompanied by outbuildings, such as a [garage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garage_\(residential\) "Garage \(residential\)") for vehicles or a [shed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shed "Shed") for gardening equipment and tools. A house may have a [backyard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backyard "Backyard") or a [front yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Front_yard "Front yard") or both, which serve as additional areas where inhabitants can relax or eat. Houses may provide "certain activities, which gradually accumulate meaning until they become homes".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:8-30)
[Joseph Rykwert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Rykwert "Joseph Rykwert") distinguished between home and house in their physicality; a house requires a building whereas a home does not.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-34) _Home_ and _house_ are often used interchangeably, although their connotations may differ: _house_ being "emotionally neutral" and _home_ evoking "personal, cognitive aspects".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:8-30)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:7-35) By the mid-18th century, the definition of home had extended beyond a house.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:11-25) "Few English words are filled with the emotional meaning of the word home".[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:10-24)
### Moveable structures
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg/250px-LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:LakeUnionHouseboat.jpg)A houseboat on [Lake Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Union "Lake Union") in [Seattle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seattle "Seattle"), [Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_\(state\) "Washington \(state\)"), US [![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kazakh_yurt.jpg/250px-Kazakh_yurt.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kazakh_yurt.jpg)A traditional Kazakh yurt on a wagon
Home as constitutionally mobile and transient has been contended by anthropologists and sociologist.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-36) A [mobile home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_home "Mobile home") (also known as a house trailer, park home, trailer, or trailer home) is a [prefabricated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefabrication "Prefabrication") structure, built in a factory on a permanently attached chassis before being transported to site (either by being towed or on a trailer). Used as permanent homes, or for holiday or temporary accommodation, they are often left permanently or semi-permanently in one place, but can be moved, and may be required to move from time to time for legal reasons. 
A [houseboat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houseboat "Houseboat") is a [boat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boat "Boat") that has been designed or modified to be used primarily as a home. Some houseboats are not motorized, because they are usually [moored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moored "Moored"), kept stationary at a fixed point and often tethered to land to provide utilities. However, many are capable of operation under their own power. Float house is a Canadian and American term for a house on a float (raft); a rough house may be called a shanty boat.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-37) In Western countries, houseboats tend to be either owned privately or rented out to holiday-goers, and on some canals in Europe, people dwell in houseboats all year round. Examples of this include, but are not limited to, Amsterdam, London, and Paris.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-38)
A traditional [yurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yurt "Yurt") or ger is a portable round tent covered with skins or [felt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felt "Felt") and used as a dwelling by several distinct [nomadic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomad "Nomad") in the [steppes of Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe"). The structure consists of an angled assembly or latticework of wood or [bamboo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamboo "Bamboo") for walls, a door frame, ribs (poles, rafters), and a wheel (crown, compression ring) possibly steam-bent. The roof structure is often self-supporting, but large yurts may have interior posts supporting the crown. The top of the wall of self-supporting yurts is prevented from spreading by means of a tension band which opposes the force of the roof ribs. Modern yurts may be permanently built on a wooden platform; they may use modern materials such as steam-bent wooden framing or metal framing, canvas or tarpaulin, plexiglass dome, wire rope, or [radiant insulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiant_barrier "Radiant barrier"). 
## Management
### Housing cooperative
This section is an excerpt from [Housing cooperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative "Housing cooperative").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housing_cooperative&action=edit)]
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg/330px-999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:999LakeShoreDriveChicago.jpg)999 N. Lake Shore Drive, a co-op–owned residential building in [Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago "Chicago"), [Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illinois "Illinois")
A [housing cooperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative "Housing cooperative"), or housing co-op, is a legal entity which owns [real estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate "Real estate") consisting of one or more residential buildings. The entity is usually a [cooperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooperative "Cooperative") or a [corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corporation "Corporation") and constitutes a form of [housing tenure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_tenure "Housing tenure"). Typically housing cooperatives are owned by shareholders but in some cases they can be owned by a non-profit organization. They are a distinctive form of home ownership that have many characteristics that differ from other residential arrangements such as [single family home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_family_home "Single family home") ownership, [condominiums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium_\(living_space\) "Condominium \(living space\)") and [renting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renting "Renting").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-39)
The cooperative is membership based, with membership granted by way of a share purchase in the cooperative. Each [shareholder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shareholder "Shareholder") in the legal entity is granted the right to occupy one housing unit. A primary advantage of the housing cooperative is the pooling of the members' resources so that their buying power is leveraged; thus lowering the cost per member in all the services and products associated with home ownership.
### Repair
This section is an excerpt from [Home repair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_repair "Home repair").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Home_repair&action=edit)]
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg/250px-FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:FEMA_-_42428_-_Home_Repair_after_Flood.jpg)A person making repairs to a house after a flood
[Home repair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_repair "Home repair") involves the diagnosis and resolution of problems in a home, and is related to home maintenance to avoid such problems. Many types of repairs are "[do it yourself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Do_it_yourself "Do it yourself")" (DIY) projects, while others may be so complicated, time-consuming or risky as to require the assistance of a qualified [handyperson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handyman "Handyman"), [property manager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_manager "Property manager"), [contractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_contractor "Independent contractor")/builder, or other professionals.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-40)
Home repair is not the same as [renovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renovation "Renovation"), although many improvements can result from repairs or maintenance. Often the costs of larger repairs will justify the alternative of investment in full-scale improvements. It may make just as much sense to upgrade a home system (with an improved one) as to repair it or incur ever-more-frequent and expensive maintenance for an inefficient, obsolete or dying system.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-41)
### Housekeeping
This section is an excerpt from [Housekeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housekeeping "Housekeeping").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housekeeping&action=edit)]
[Housekeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housekeeping "Housekeeping") is the management and routine support activities of running and maintaining an organized physical institution occupied or used by people, like a [house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House"), [ship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship "Ship"), [hospital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital "Hospital") or [factory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factory "Factory"), such as [cleaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleaning "Cleaning"), tidying/organizing, [cooking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooking "Cooking"), [shopping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shopping "Shopping"), and bill payment. These tasks may be performed by members of the [household](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Household "Household"), or by [persons hired for the purpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_worker "Domestic worker"). This is a more broad role than a [cleaner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleaner "Cleaner"), who is focused only on the cleaning aspect.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-Housekeeping_House_Keeper_vs_Cleaner_differentiation-42) The term is also used to refer to the money allocated for such use.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-Housekeeping_oxford-43) By extension, it may also refer to an office or a corporation, as well as the [maintenance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housekeeping_\(computing\) "Housekeeping \(computing\)") of [computer storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_data_storage "Computer data storage") systems.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-44)
The basic concept can be divided into domestic housekeeping, for private households, and institutional housekeeping for commercial and other institutions providing shelter or lodging, such as hotels, resorts, inns, boarding houses, dormitories, hospitals and prisons.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-Housekeeping_Collins-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-Housekeeping_Hospitals-46) There are related concepts in industry known as workplace housekeeping and Industrial housekeeping, which are part of occupational health and safety processes. 
A [housekeeper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housekeeper_\(domestic_worker\) "Housekeeper \(domestic worker\)") is a person employed to manage a household[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-47) and the [domestic staff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domestic_staff "Domestic staff"). According to the 1861 Victorian era _[Mrs. Beeton's Book of Household Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mrs._Beeton%27s_Book_of_Household_Management "Mrs. Beeton's Book of Household Management")_ , the housekeeper is second in command in the house and "except in large establishments, where there is a house steward, the housekeeper must consider herself as the immediate representative of her mistress".[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-48)
## Tenure
This section is an excerpt from [Housing tenure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_tenure "Housing tenure").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Housing_tenure&action=edit)]
[Housing tenure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_tenure "Housing tenure") is a financial arrangement and ownership structure under which someone has the right to live in a [house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House") or [apartment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartment "Apartment"). The most frequent forms are [tenancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leasehold_estate "Leasehold estate"), in which [rent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renting "Renting") is paid by the occupant to a [landlord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlord "Landlord"), and [owner-occupancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owner-occupancy "Owner-occupancy"), where the occupant owns their own home. Mixed forms of tenure are also possible. 
The basic forms of tenure can be subdivided, for example an owner-occupier may own a house outright, or it may be [mortgaged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortgage_law "Mortgage law"). In the case of tenancy, the landlord may be a private individual, a [non-profit organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-profit_organization "Non-profit organization") such as a [housing association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_association "Housing association"), or a government body, as in [public housing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_housing "Public housing"). 
[Surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_survey "Statistical survey") used in [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") research frequently include questions about housing tenure, because it is a useful [proxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_\(statistics\) "Proxy \(statistics\)") for income or wealth, and people are less reluctant to give information about it.
### Owner-occupancy
This section is an excerpt from [Owner-occupancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owner-occupancy "Owner-occupancy").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Owner-occupancy&action=edit)]
[Owner-occupancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owner-occupancy "Owner-occupancy") or home-ownership is a form of [housing tenure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_tenure "Housing tenure") in which a person, called the owner-occupier, owner-occupant, or home owner, owns the home in which they live.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-49) The home can be a [house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House "House"), such as a [single-family house](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-family_detached_home "Single-family detached home"), an [apartment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartment "Apartment"), [condominium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condominium "Condominium"), or a [housing cooperative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing_cooperative "Housing cooperative"). In addition to providing [housing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Housing "Housing"), owner-occupancy also functions as a [real estate investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_estate_investing "Real estate investing").

### Squatting
This section is an excerpt from [Squatting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting "Squatting").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Squatting&action=edit)]
[Squatting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting "Squatting") is the action of occupying an abandoned or unoccupied area of land or a building (usually residential) that the squatter does not [own, rent or otherwise have lawful permission to use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_ownership_and_tenure "Land ownership and tenure"). The [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") estimated in 2003 that there were one billion slum residents and squatters globally. Squatting is practiced worldwide, typically when people find empty buildings or land to occupy for housing. 
In [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_countries "Developing countries") and [least developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_developed_countries "Least developed countries"), [shanty towns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanty_towns "Shanty towns") often begin as squatted settlements. In African cities such as [Lagos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagos "Lagos"), much of the population lives in [slums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slums "Slums"). There are [pavement dwellers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pavement_dwellers "Pavement dwellers") in India and in Hong Kong as well as [rooftop slums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rooftop_slum "Rooftop slum"). [Informal settlements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_settlements "Informal settlements") in Latin America are known by names such as [villa miseria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villa_miseria "Villa miseria") (Argentina), [pueblos jóvenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pueblos_j%C3%B3venes "Pueblos jóvenes") (Peru) and [asentamientos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asentamiento "Asentamiento") irregulares (Guatemala, Uruguay). In Brazil, there are [favelas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Favelas "Favelas") in the major cities and land-based movements in rural areas. 
In [industrialized countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrialized_countries "Industrialized countries"), there are often residential squats and also [left-wing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-wing "Left-wing") squatting movements, which can be [anarchist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchist "Anarchist"), [autonomist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomist "Autonomist") or [socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist "Socialist") in nature, for example [in the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting_in_the_United_States "Squatting in the United States"). Oppositional movements from the 1960s and 1970s created freespaces in Denmark, [the Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting_in_the_Netherlands "Squatting in the Netherlands") and the [self-managed social centres of Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-managed_social_centers_in_Italy "Self-managed social centers in Italy"). Each local situation determines the context: in [England and Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting_in_England_and_Wales "Squatting in England and Wales"), there were estimated to be 50,000 squatters in the late 1970s; in Athens, Greece, there are [refugee squats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens_refugee_squats "Athens refugee squats"). In [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squatting_in_Spain "Squatting in Spain") and the US, the 2010s saw many new squats following the [2008 financial crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_financial_crisis "2008 financial crisis").
### Homelessness
Main article: [Homelessness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homelessness "Homelessness")
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/250px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg)Homeless people in [San'ya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San%27ya "San'ya") district, [Tokyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokyo "Tokyo"), Japan
The state of being without a home can occur in many ways,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:4-50) ranging from the upheavals of [natural disasters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_disaster "Natural disaster"),[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-51) fraud, theft, [arson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arson "Arson"), or war-related destruction, to the more common voluntary sale, loss for one or more occupants on relationship breakdown, [expropriation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eminent_domain "Eminent domain") by government or legislated cause, repossession or [foreclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreclosure "Foreclosure") to pay secured debts, [eviction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eviction "Eviction") by [landlords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlord "Landlord"), disposal by time-limited means – [lease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lease "Lease"), or absolute gift. Jurisdiction-dependent means of home loss include [adverse possession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_possession "Adverse possession"), unpaid [property taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_taxation "Property taxation") and corruption such as in circumstances of a [failed state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Failed_state "Failed state"). 
[Personal insolvency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_insolvency "Personal insolvency"), development or sustaining of [mental illness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_disorder "Mental disorder") or severe physical incapacity without affordable [domestic care](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Care_work "Care work") commonly lead to a change of home. The underlying character of a home may be debased by [structural defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_integrity_and_failure "Structural integrity and failure"), natural [subsidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsidence "Subsidence"), [neglect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_maintenance "Property maintenance") or [soil contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_contamination "Soil contamination"). [Refugees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refugee "Refugee") are people who have fled their homes due to violence or [persecution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persecution "Persecution"). They may seek temporary housing in a shelter, or claim [asylum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_of_asylum "Right of asylum") in another country in an attempt to relocate permanently. A dysfunctional home life commonly precipitates one's homelessness.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:4-50)
The dichotomy between home and homelessness is to the extent that the concept of home, scholars have said, is dependent on homelessness: "in a sense, without homelessness, we would not be concerned with what home means".[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:4-50)
## Anthropogenic significance
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg/250px-Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Welcome_home_our_heroes_MET_DP873465.jpg)A celebratory poster for soldiers and marines returning home
The connection between humans and dwelling is profound, such that, the likes of [Gaston Bachelard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaston_Bachelard "Gaston Bachelard") and [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger") consider it an "essential characteristic" of humanity.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:7-35) A home is generally a place that is close to the heart of the owner, and can become a prized possession. It has been argued that psychologically "The strongest sense of home commonly coincides geographically with a dwelling. Usually, the sense of home attenuates as one moves away from that point, but it does not do so in a fixed or regular way."[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-52) A person's conception of home can be dependent on congealing conditions, such as culture, geography or emotion; the sense of being at home may be contingent upon the presence of multiple emotions, such as joy, sorrow, nostalgia and pride.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-54) Further psychological interperation contends that homes serve the purpose of satisfying identity-based desires and expression and that it functions as a "symbol of the self", bound to the events of one's life.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:9-28)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-55) [Emmanuel Levinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Levinas "Emmanuel Levinas") wrote of home as where, upon seclusion from the greater world, a sense of self can be regained.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-56)
There exist many connotations regarding the concept of a home, including of security, identity, ritual and socialisation, varied definitions and residents may associate their home with meanings, emotions, experiences and relationships.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:0-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-57) Home has been described as an "[essentially contested concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essentially_contested_concept "Essentially contested concept")".[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-58) Common connotations of home are espoused by both those with or without a home.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18) It is the sociality and action of homes which some scholars have said conditions a house in to a home, which is, according to Gram-Hanssen, "a [phenomenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenon "Phenomenon") made by its residents".[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-59) Dysfunctional sociality may negate the sense of a residence being a home whereas the physical contents may endow the sense; alienated from home one may feel "metaphorically homeless".[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-61)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-62) Romantic or nostalgic notions are typical in the conceptions of "ideal homes", at once a cultural and individual concept.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:2-23)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-63) An ideal working-class home in [Postwar Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postwar_Britain_\(1945%E2%80%931979\) "Postwar Britain \(1945–1979\)") was one of comfort and cleanliness, plentiful with food and compassion.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-64)
In modern America, an owned house has greater cachet as a home than other residences; debate exists as to if a rooming house can provide a home.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-65) Some housing scholars have contended that a conflation of house and home is the result of popular media and capitalist interest.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:2-23) Differing cultures may perceive the concept of a home differently, ascribing less value to the privacy of a residence or the residence itself – although housing issues have been seen as of great concern to immigrants.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-66) The home can render to men and women in significant differences: men conditioned to experience great control and little labour and vice versa for women; homelessness too can be subject to differences per gender.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:4-50) Sociologist Shelley Mallett preposed the idea of home as abstractions: space, feeling, [praxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxis_\(process\) "Praxis \(process\)") or "a way of being in the world".[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21) Abstract notions of home are present in the proverb "A house is not a home".[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:4-50)
[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dream_Home_Wiki_Loves_Africa_2022_By_Green_Wilfred_Somoni.webm "Play video")[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dream_Home_Wiki_Loves_Africa_2022_By_Green_Wilfred_Somoni.webm)A video showing a child in [Port Harcourt, Nigeria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Harcourt "Port Harcourt") aspiring for a future home
Since it can be said that humans are generally creatures of [habit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habituation "Habituation"), the state of a person's home has been known to physiologically influence their [behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behavior "Behavior"), [emotions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotion "Emotion"), and overall [mental health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_health "Mental health").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-67) [Marianne Gullestad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marianne_Gullestad "Marianne Gullestad") wrote of the home as the center of and as an attempt to amalgamate everyday life; one's conduct there, she said, can reflect greater culture or social values, such as [gender roles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_role "Gender role") insinuating the home to be the domain of women.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-69) To be [homesick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homesickness "Homesickness") is to desire [belonging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belongingness "Belongingness"), said [Zygmunt Bauman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygmunt_Bauman "Zygmunt Bauman").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18) Places like homes can trigger self-reflection, thoughts about who someone is or used to be or who they might become.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-70) These types of reflections also occur in places where there is a collective historical identity, such as [Gettysburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg "Battle of Gettysburg") or [Ground Zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_Zero "Ground Zero").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-71) The time spent with one's home is a considerable element in establishing one's attachment.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21) Those without significant time spent of their life in a residence often struggle to consider home as a feature of residences.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:5-18) The perception of one's home can extend beyond the residence itself, to their neighbourhood, family, workplace or nation and one may feel as though they have multiple homes; to have felt at home beyond residence can be a significant element in one's appraisal of their life, a time in which notions of home, it has been observed, are more profound.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:1-21)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-72)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-73)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:3-68) The connection between home and family is pertinent, to the extent that some scholars consider the terms to be synonymous.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home#cite_note-:2-23)
