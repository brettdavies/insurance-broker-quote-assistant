# What Is Texas Fair Plan Insurance and What Does It Cover?
Learn how Texas Fair Plan Insurance provides coverage for high-risk properties, who qualifies, what it includes, and how to apply or handle claims.
[ ![](https://legalclarity.org/wp-content/uploads/fly-images/243250/1739632772-8620-64x64.png) ](https://legalclarity.org/users/8620/profile/insurance-insights)
[Insurance Insights](https://legalclarity.org/users/8620/profile/insurance-insights)
Published Mar 16, 2025
Homeowners in Texas who struggle to find property insurance through the private market may have an alternative. The Texas Fair Plan Insurance provides coverage for those unable to secure a policy due to high-risk factors such as location or past claims history. While not a replacement for standard homeowners insurance, it serves as a last resort for eligible residents. 
Understanding how this plan works and what it covers can help homeowners determine if it meets their needs. 
## Regulatory Oversight
The Texas Fair Plan Insurance is regulated by the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI), which ensures the program operates within state guidelines and serves homeowners who cannot obtain coverage through private insurers. Unlike standard homeowners policies underwritten by private companies with varying risk appetites, the Texas Fair Plan is a residual market insurer, existing solely to provide coverage when no other options are available. TDI oversees its financial stability, rate structures, and policy provisions to ensure it remains solvent while fulfilling its mandate. 
Rates and policy terms are determined through actuarial analysis and regulatory approval rather than competitive market forces. Premiums reflect the heightened risk associated with insuring properties private carriers have declined. While the plan does not receive direct state funding, it is backed by assessments on private insurers operating in Texas, which may be called upon to cover deficits if claims exceed collected premiums. This structure maintains financial viability while limiting its impact on the broader insurance market. 
## Eligibility Requirements
To qualify, applicants must first demonstrate they have been denied coverage by at least two licensed insurance companies actively writing policies in Texas. This requirement ensures the program remains a last-resort option rather than a competitor to private insurers. Coverage may be denied due to a property’s location, age, condition, or the homeowner’s claims history. If an applicant has received nonrenewal notices or declinations for these reasons, they may be eligible for a Fair Plan policy. 
The property itself must meet specific insurability standards. Homes in poor condition, vacant properties, or those used for commercial purposes are ineligible. An inspection may be required to confirm the dwelling meets basic structural and safety criteria, such as a functional roof, secure windows and doors, and proper electrical, plumbing, and heating systems. If deficiencies are found, policy issuance may be contingent on necessary repairs. 
## Policy Coverage
Texas Fair Plan Insurance provides limited coverage compared to standard homeowners policies, focusing primarily on protecting the structure of the home. It covers damage caused by specific perils, including fire, lightning, windstorms, hail, explosion, and vandalism. Unlike many traditional policies, it does not automatically include coverage for theft, water damage, or liability, requiring homeowners to seek additional policies or endorsements to fill these gaps. 
Coverage limits vary based on the property’s value and associated risk. While private insurers often offer replacement cost coverage, Fair Plan policies typically provide actual cash value (ACV) settlements, factoring in depreciation and potentially leaving homeowners responsible for part of the repair or rebuilding costs. Deductibles also affect out-of-pocket expenses, with policyholders selecting from set amounts that influence both premium costs and claim reimbursements. 
## Application Steps
Applying for Texas Fair Plan Insurance requires proof of prior coverage denials. Once a homeowner has documented at least two refusals from licensed insurers, they must work with an authorized insurance agent to submit an application. The Texas Fair Plan Association (TFPA) does not sell policies directly to consumers, making a knowledgeable agent essential to navigating the process. Agents collect required information, including property details, ownership verification, and underwriting documentation, to ensure the application meets program criteria. 
The home must meet minimum insurability standards. If an inspection is required, the homeowner may need to address deficiencies before coverage is approved. Standard submissions include photographs of the property, proof of prior insurance history, and a completed underwriting questionnaire detailing existing risks. The TFPA evaluates this data to determine coverage levels and premiums based on location, construction type, and potential hazards. 
## Claim Protocol
Filing a claim requires prompt action and thorough documentation. Policyholders must notify the Texas Fair Plan Association (TFPA) as soon as they become aware of damage. Delays can impact the claim outcome, as insurers may question whether additional damage resulted from neglect or failure to mitigate further harm. Claims can be initiated online, over the phone, or through an insurance agent. Homeowners should have their policy number, date of loss, and a description of the damage readily available. 
Once a claim is filed, TFPA assigns an adjuster to assess the damage. The adjuster conducts an inspection, reviews repair estimates, and determines the payout based on policy terms. Because Texas Fair Plan policies often provide actual cash value (ACV) rather than replacement cost coverage, the final payout may be reduced by depreciation. Policyholders are encouraged to obtain independent repair estimates to compare with the insurer’s assessment. If discrepancies arise, homeowners can request a reevaluation or submit additional documentation supporting their claim. 
## Denial and Dispute
If a claim is denied, policyholders can challenge the decision. TFPA must provide a written explanation outlining the reasons for denial, which may include policy exclusions, insufficient documentation, or failure to meet reporting deadlines. Homeowners should carefully review this letter and compare it with their policy to identify potential errors or misinterpretations. If the denial is due to missing or incomplete information, submitting additional evidence such as contractor estimates or photographs may prompt reconsideration. 
For disputes that cannot be resolved directly with TFPA, policyholders can pursue mediation or file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI), which may conduct a regulatory review to determine if the insurer acted in accordance with state guidelines. In some cases, homeowners may opt for legal action or hire a public adjuster to negotiate a fair settlement. Keeping thorough records of all communications and claim-related expenses strengthens a homeowner’s position when disputing a denied or underpaid claim.
