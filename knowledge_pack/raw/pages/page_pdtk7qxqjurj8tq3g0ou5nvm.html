The national average cost of car insurance over the course of an entire year was $3,017 in 2023, about $251 a month.

That's a decent chunk of change: it's more than the average American spends on clothing. And it amounts to 4% of the median salary in America.

Car insurance rates vary by state and provider, and are impacted by your driving record, credit score, and other factors. It's also a good idea to regularly check rates from car insurance providers beyond your current one to see if a better deal is available.

To help shoppers navigate the auto insurance market, Motley Fool Money has compiled:

  • The average cost of car insurance in all 50 states
  • The cost of minimum and standard auto insurance in each state
  • The cheapest and most expensive car insurance providers in each state
  • Tips to reduce your auto insurance premium

The national average annual cost of car insurance: $3,017

That national average annual cost of car insurance in 2023 was $3,017, according to Quadrant. That amounts to about $251 a month.

Auto insurance has gotten more expensive over the past three years. The average annual auto insurance premium in 2021 cost $2,640, or about $220 a month.

Year National average auto insurance payment
2021 $2,640
2022 $2,875
2023 $3,017
Data source: Quadrant (2023).

While a variety of factors influence your individual car insurance rate, like age, location, driving history, and credit score, the overall cost of car insurance has risen in recent years due to repairs becoming more expensive and hazardous weather that's more frequent and intense, leading to vehicle damage.

The average cost of car insurance by coverage

The type of car insurance coverage a car owner opts to purchase through their auto insurance policy has a significant impact on the price of that policy.

Minimum coverage, which is coverage that states require car owners to purchase, is significantly cheaper than more comprehensive coverage.

The average cost of minimum auto insurance coverage in 2023 was $787 over the entire year, roughly $66 a month. More comprehensive coverage cost $3,296 for the year, roughly $275 a month.

Year National average auto insurance payment Minimum coverage Standard coverage
2021 $2,640 $761 $2,909
2022 $2,875 $783 $3,137
2023 $3,017 $787 $3,296
Data source: Quadrant (2023).

Minimum auto insurance coverage generally includes bodily injury liability and property damage liability. That coverage is often grouped together as liability coverage. They help cover the costs of injuries and property damage caused to another person during an accident.

Standard coverage includes liability coverage and all or most of the following:

  • Uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage
  • Comprehensive coverage
  • Collision coverage
  • Medical payment coverage
  • Personal injury protection (PIP)

How to lower your car insurance premium

To get a better car insurance rate, it's necessary to understand how the price of auto insurance policies are set. Most companies look at these factors when setting your rate through their own insurance score system:

  • Driving record: New and younger drivers and those who have gotten into major accidents, received a DUI, or been booked for speeding tickets will have higher auto insurance rates than drivers with a clean record.
  • Driving frequency: Policyholders that drive more will pay more for auto insurance.
  • Location: Living in an area with higher rates of theft and accidents can increase the cost of auto insurance. Locations where severe weather that can damage cars, like hail, is more common can also have more expensive policies.
  • Gender: Women have historically been safer drivers than men and as a result may receive a better car insurance rate.
  • Automobile model: More expensive cars are more expensive to insure. Cars with certain safety features may qualify for discounts.

With those factors in mind, here are some ways to save on auto insurance:

  • Shop around: Whenever your policy comes up for renewal, make a habit of looking at rates other insurance companies offer.
  • Bundle auto and homeowners insurance: If you're a homeowner, bundling auto and homeowners insurance can net savings.
  • Explore paying per mile: If you aren't driving to work and otherwise minimally use your car, it may make sense to look at whether a pay-per-mile policy would lower your premium.
  • Look for discounts: Many auto insurance companies offer discounts for actions taken that lower risk. These include taking a defensive driving course, parking in a secure garage if you live in an urban area, and driving a vehicle with safety features like anti-lock brakes.
  • Maintain good credit: Many auto insurance providers use credit history as a factor in calculating auto insurance scores. Data shows that good credit can net big savings.
  • Keep a clean driving record: Getting a DUI, being in a major accident, and accumulating speeding tickets can make auto insurance much more expensive. Taking an Uber or taxi after a night out, focusing on the road, and staying within the speed limit are well worth it when considering the financial consequences of being pulled over or getting in a wreck.
